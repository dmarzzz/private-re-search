# The search problem: a formalization, and why it differs for humans and agents

## 1. Introduction

"Search" names at least three distinct research programs that rarely cite one another. In classical artificial intelligence it is the foundational paradigm of problem solving: finding a path through a space of states from an initial configuration to a goal ([Newell and Simon, 1972](https://archive.org/details/humanproblemsolv0000newe); [Russell and Norvig, 2021](https://aima.cs.berkeley.edu/)). In economics and statistics it is the theory of when to stop sampling from an uncertain environment, trading the value of one more observation against its cost ([Stigler, 1961](https://doi.org/10.1086/258464); [Wald, 1945](https://doi.org/10.1214/aoms/1177731118)). In information science it is the problem of ranking documents in a corpus by their probability of relevance to an expressed need ([Maron and Kuhns, 1960](https://doi.org/10.1145/321033.321035); [Robertson, 1977](https://doi.org/10.1108/eb026647)). A fourth tradition, the empirical study of human information behavior, describes how real, bounded people actually conduct search ([Pirolli and Card, 1999](https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-295X.106.4.643); [Bates, 1989](https://doi.org/10.1108/eb024320)).

These programs developed largely in parallel, with distinct vocabularies: states versus draws versus documents, goal tests versus reservation values versus relevance judgments. The rise of large language model (LLM) agents that issue queries, read documents, and decide when they have found enough ([Yao et al., 2023](https://arxiv.org/abs/2210.03629); [Jin et al., 2025](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.09516)) makes the lack of a common frame costly. We cannot say precisely how an LLM "deep-research" agent differs from a human searcher if the agent is described in the language of reinforcement learning and the human in the language of foraging ecology, with no shared formalism.

This report does four things. First (§2), it gives a single unified formalization of the search problem and shows that the AI state-space formulation, the decision-theoretic optimal-stopping formulation, and the IR relevance/ranking formulation are three projections of that one frame. Second (§3), it specializes the frame to information search over a corpus or the web, recovering relevance, ranking, and the Cranfield/TREC evaluation paradigm. Third (§4 and §5), it instantiates the frame for two kinds of searcher, the bounded human and the LLM agent, making each one's objective, cost model, action space, and stopping rule explicit. Fourth (§6), it contrasts the two formally, axis by axis, so that "who is searching" becomes a parameter of one problem rather than a choice between two unrelated problems. We close (§7) with open problems, including what privacy-preserving search infrastructure (IP anonymization for queries) implies once the searcher is an agent rather than a person.

The unifying claim throughout is normative in the sense of [Russell and Subramanian (1995)](https://doi.org/10.1613/jair.133): neither human nor agent achieves perfect rationality, so the meaningful question is which is closer to *bounded-optimal* for its architecture and environment.

## 2. The general search problem

### 2.1 The unified tuple

We model search as a tuple

```
P = < B, b0, T, A, RESULT, U, c, π, σ >
```

with the following components.

- **B**, an information/belief-state space, with a distinguished **initial state b0 ∈ B**. A state b summarizes everything the searcher has observed and inferred so far. In an idealized fully-observable problem b is just a world state; in general it is a belief state (a distribution over latent variables, including the as-yet-unknown value of unexamined options).
- **T**, the *information need* or task, which the searcher holds and which defines what "done" and "good" mean. T is fixed in the simplest models but may itself be uncertain or evolving (see §4).
- **A(b)**, the set of actions (operators, queries, draws, computations) available in state b.
- **RESULT(b, a)**, a transition model returning the successor state (or a distribution over successor states) after taking action a in state b. An action yields an *observation* o that updates the belief state.
- **U(b, T)**, a utility or value function measuring how well state b satisfies need T. We write the relevance of a particular retrieved object d to need T as **rel(d, T)**; U typically aggregates relevance over what has been gathered.
- **c(b, a)** ≥ 0, a cost function for taking action a in state b, denominated in the searcher's scarce resource(s).
- **π**, the *policy*: a (possibly stochastic) mapping from states to actions, π(b) ∈ A(b), that governs which action to take next, including reformulation, deepening, and backtracking.
- **σ**, the *stopping rule*: a predicate σ(b) ∈ {continue, stop} that terminates the episode and commits to an answer or selection.

A *search episode* is a trajectory b0 →a1 b1 →a2 … →ak bk with σ(bk) = stop. The searcher's overall objective is to choose π and σ to maximize expected net value,

```
J(π, σ) = E [ U(bτ, T) − Σ_{t<τ} c(b_{t−1}, a_t) ],
```

where τ is the (random) stopping time induced by σ. This single objective, *expected terminal value minus accumulated search cost*, is the spine of the report. Everything below is a specialization of it.

Two levels operate here. The **object level** chooses external actions (which query to issue, which document to read). The **meta level** chooses whether to keep deliberating/searching at all: it owns σ. This object/meta distinction, made precise by [Russell and Wefald (1991)](https://doi.org/10.1016/0004-3702(91)90015-C) and [Horvitz (1987)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1304.2759), is what lets the three classical traditions slot into one frame: AI search is mostly an object-level theory, optimal-stopping is mostly a meta-level theory, and IR ranking is a theory of how a single object-level action's outputs should be ordered.

### 2.2 Projection (a): AI state-space search

[Russell and Norvig (2021, ch. 3)](https://aima.cs.berkeley.edu/) define a problem as the five-tuple (initial state, actions, transition model RESULT(s,a), goal test, step/path cost). This is P with U specialized to a Boolean goal test, GOAL(b) ∈ {0,1}, and the stopping rule σ(b) = stop iff GOAL(b). Cost c is additive along the path, g = Σ c(s,a,s'), and an optimal solution minimizes g over paths that reach a goal. The conceptual root is [Newell and Simon's (1972)](https://archive.org/details/humanproblemsolv0000newe) problem-space hypothesis (intelligent behavior is heuristic search through a space of symbolic states reached by operators), operationalized in the General Problem Solver via means-ends analysis ([Newell, Shaw and Simon, 1959](https://www.semanticscholar.org/), reported at the IFIP Congress, pp. 256–264).

The optimization content lives in the policy π. Best-first search with [A* (Hart, Nilsson and Raphael, 1968)](https://doi.org/10.1109/TSSC.1968.300136) expands the open node minimizing f(n) = g(n) + h(n), where g(n) is the cost incurred from b0 to n and h(n) is a heuristic estimate of the cost-to-go to a goal. If h is *admissible* (h ≤ h*, never overestimating true remaining cost) A* returns an optimal path; with a consistent h it is optimally efficient. The decomposition f = g + h, *sunk cost plus estimated value-to-go*, is the object-level analogue of the meta-level cost/benefit tradeoff that recurs throughout. The heuristic h is exactly the place where domain knowledge enters; with h ≡ 0 one recovers uniform-cost (Dijkstra) search.

### 2.3 Projection (b): decision-theoretic search with optimal stopping

Here B is dominated by the searcher's belief about the value distribution of options not yet examined, and A is "draw one more option at cost c" versus "stop." The objective J is the expected best value found minus total search cost.

[Stigler (1961)](https://doi.org/10.1086/258464) gave the founding, *non-sequential* version: fix a sample size n in advance to minimize E[best of n draws] + n·c, choosing n* where the marginal expected improvement equals c. The *sequential* refinement replaces a fixed n with a reservation value. In [McCall's (1970)](https://doi.org/10.2307/1879403) labor-search model the agent accepts the first option clearing a reservation threshold w̄ defined by the search cost and the continuation value; equivalently σ stops iff the best option in hand exceeds the value of one more expected draw ([Lippman and McCall, 1976](https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1465-7295.1976.tb00386.x)). [Weitzman's (1979)](https://doi.org/10.2307/1912769) "Pandora's Box" generalizes this to heterogeneous, costly-to-inspect options: assign each box i a reservation value z_i solving c_i = E[max(0, v_i − z_i)], open boxes in descending z_i, and stop when the best reward in hand exceeds the highest z_i among unopened boxes. This index rule is the cleanest statement of σ for heterogeneous information sources and maps directly onto an agent deciding which of several candidate searches or documents to "open" next.

The statistical lineage gives the same shape for a different terminal decision. [Wald's (1945)](https://doi.org/10.1214/aoms/1177731118) sequential probability ratio test continues sampling while the likelihood ratio L_n sits between thresholds B and A, stopping to accept or reject otherwise; [Wald and Wolfowitz (1948)](https://doi.org/10.1214/aoms/1177730197) proved this minimizes expected sample size for fixed error rates. The decision-theoretic glue is the *value of information*: [Howard (1966)](https://doi.org/10.1109/TSSC.1966.300074) defines EVPI = E[max_a u] − max_a E[u], and the [Raiffa and Schlaifer (1961)](https://www.hbs.edu/) machinery says take another costly sample iff its expected value of sample information (EVSI) exceeds its cost. The pure ordinal benchmark is the secretary problem, whose no-recall optimal rule rejects the first ~37% of candidates then takes the next record ([Ferguson, 1989](https://doi.org/10.1214/ss/1177012493)). Across all of these, σ is a *reservation-value / marginal-value* rule: stop when the expected marginal benefit of continuing drops below its marginal cost.

### 2.4 Projection (c): the IR relevance/ranking model

The IR tradition fixes a single object-level action, "run query q against corpus D", and asks how its output should be ordered. [Maron and Kuhns (1960)](https://doi.org/10.1145/321033.321035) treated relevance as a random event and scored each document by its estimated probability of relevance, the first explicit objective function for search. [Robertson's (1977)](https://doi.org/10.1108/eb026647) Probability Ranking Principle (PRP) makes the optimality claim precise: ranking documents by decreasing P(R = 1 | d, q) maximizes expected retrieval effectiveness under stated independence and utility assumptions. In our frame, the PRP is the optimal *presentation order* of the candidates surfaced by one action, given that the downstream stopping rule σ scans the ranked list top-down (see §4.6).

Concrete scoring functions are estimators of rel(d, T). The Vector Space Model ranks by cosine similarity of tf-idf vectors ([Salton, Wong and Yang, 1975](https://doi.org/10.1145/361219.361220)), with inverse document frequency idf(t) = log(N/n_t) supplying term specificity ([Sparck Jones, 1972](https://doi.org/10.1108/eb026526), "A Statistical Interpretation of Term Specificity and Its Application in Retrieval"). The Probabilistic Relevance Framework derives the Robertson–Sparck Jones weight from the PRP under term independence ([Robertson and Sparck Jones, 1976](https://doi.org/10.1002/asi.4630270302)) and, via a two-Poisson eliteness model, yields BM25 ([Robertson and Zaragoza, 2009](https://doi.org/10.1561/1500000019)):

```
BM25(q,d) = Σ_{t∈q} IDF(t) · tf · (k1+1) / ( tf + k1·(1 − b + b·|d|/avgdl) ).
```

The language-modeling view inverts the conditioning: rank by the query likelihood P(q | M_d) under a document language model with smoothing ([Ponte and Croft, 1998](https://doi.org/10.1145/290941.291008); [Zhai and Lafferty, 2004](https://doi.org/10.1145/984321.984322)). The most general classical statement is risk minimization: rank by the negative KL divergence between a query model M_q and a document model M_d, which recovers the vector-space, classical-probabilistic, and query-likelihood models as special cases ([Lafferty and Zhai, 2001](https://doi.org/10.1145/383952.383970)). All of these are instances of a single move in P: estimate rel(d, T) and let π/σ act on the estimate.

Relevance itself is not a scalar. [Saracevic (1975](https://doi.org/10.1002/asi.4630260604), [2007)](https://doi.org/10.1002/asi.20682) and [Mizzaro (1997)](https://doi.org/10.1002/(SICI)1097-4571(199709)48:9%3C810::AID-ASI6%3E3.0.CO;2-U) describe relevance as stratified and multidimensional (system/algorithmic, topical, cognitive, situational, affective) and dynamic over a search episode. This is the crucial caveat for the contrast in §6: rel(d, T) that a ranking function optimizes (system/topical relevance) is not the same stratum as the situational/cognitive relevance the searcher actually cares about.

### 2.5 Why these are one problem

The three projections differ in which component of P they elaborate and which they hold trivial. AI search elaborates B, A, RESULT, and π over a path, with U a Boolean goal and σ trivial (stop at goal). Optimal-stopping theory elaborates U, c, and especially σ over an uncertain value distribution, with A minimal ("draw / stop"). IR ranking elaborates rel and the ordering of one action's output, holding the multi-step process implicit. The unifying recognition, and the reason a single objective J(π, σ) governs all three, is that each is solving "choose actions and a stopping time to maximize expected terminal value minus accumulated cost," differing only in what is uncertain and what is expensive. The bridge between (a) and (b) is explicit: A*'s f = g + h is an object-level reservation-value computation, and the meta-level version of "expand the node with the best estimated value-to-go per unit cost" is precisely value-of-computation reasoning ([Russell and Wefald, 1991](https://doi.org/10.1016/0004-3702(91)90015-C)). The bridge between (b) and (c) is that the PRP is a decision-theoretic optimality result ([Robertson, 1977](https://doi.org/10.1108/eb026647)): rank by probability of relevance because that is the ordering that maximizes expected utility under a top-down examination model.

## 3. Information search as a specialization

Specialize P to finding information in a corpus or the web. The state b is the searcher's evolving knowledge plus the evidence gathered so far. The environment is a corpus D of documents (web pages, passages, records). The need T is an information need, typically expressed only through a query q that is a lossy projection of T (§4.2). The action set A includes at least {issue/reformulate a query q, examine a surrogate (snippet/title), open and read a document d, stop}. The transition RESULT returns observations: a ranked result list for a query, the contents of a document for a read. The value function aggregates relevance, U(b, T) = aggregate over gathered d of rel(d, T), where rel is estimated by a scoring function from §2.4. Cost c is the resource spent per action (time and attention for a person; tokens, compute, latency, and money for an agent, §5). The policy π chooses the next query or document; the stopping rule σ decides when enough relevant material has been gathered.

The decomposition into a *retrieve* step (a query action that returns candidates ranked by rel) and an *examine/read* step (acting on those candidates) is the information-search instantiation of the object level. The PRP says the retrieve step should rank by P(R | d, q); the examine step is where the stopping rule of §2.3 bites. This is the structure that both classical IR systems and modern retrieval-augmented agents share, which is why §5 can describe RAG as "the retrieve step made differentiable."

Evaluation in this specialization is operationalized by the **Cranfield paradigm**: a test collection is a triple (D, Q, J) of documents, topics/queries, and relevance judgments (qrels), and effectiveness is measured by metrics such as precision, recall, MAP, and nDCG, abstracting the user out of the loop ([Cleverdon, 1967](https://doi.org/10.1108/eb050097)). [TREC](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262220736/trec/) scaled this with pooled judgments across many systems ([Voorhees and Harman, 2005](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262220736/trec/)). Cranfield-style evaluation scores the *ranking* (the retrieve step's output) against fixed relevance, not the searcher's terminal decision, a point that becomes central when the searcher is an agent producing a synthesized answer rather than a ranked list (§7).

## 4. The human searcher

### 4.1 The objective: rate maximization under bounded rationality

The dominant formal model of the human searcher is Information Foraging Theory ([Pirolli and Card, 1999](https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-295X.106.4.643)), which imports optimal foraging from behavioral ecology. The human is a forager maximizing the *rate* of information gain,

```
R = G / (T_B + T_W),
```

where G is information value gained, T_B is between-patch time (navigating to and locating sources) and T_W is within-patch time (extracting value from a source). Information *patches* are localized collections: a site, a result list, a document. A diet model ranks sources by profitability (value per handling time) to decide which to pursue or ignore. In our frame this specializes U to cumulative gain G and makes the cost model time-denominated, with the objective being a rate rather than a one-shot net value.

This rate objective is itself a bounded-rationality model. [Simon (1955](https://doi.org/10.2307/1884852), [1956)](https://doi.org/10.1037/h0042769) argued that real agents do not optimize over all alternatives; they use simplified, resource-feasible procedures because cognition, time, and attention are scarce. Foraging is exactly such a procedure: it acts on local, cheap estimates rather than on global optimization.

### 4.2 The query is not the need: ASK, berrypicking, exploratory search

A defining human constraint is that T is not directly accessible even to the searcher. [Belkin, Oddy and Brooks (1982)](https://doi.org/10.1108/eb026722) ("ASK for Information Retrieval, Part I") frame the information need as an *Anomalous State of Knowledge*: a gap the user cannot precisely specify, so the query q is a degraded projection of an ill-formed need. In P this is a large noise/uncertainty term on the query side; classical IR's assumption of a well-formed request is rejected.

Because T is underspecified, it also *evolves*. [Bates's (1989)](https://doi.org/10.1108/eb024320) berrypicking model rejects the static one-query/one-result-set picture: the need and query change as the searcher encounters information, so search is a connected sequence q1 → d1 → q2 → d2 … over a shifting need, and the "answer" is the trail of berries collected rather than a single retrieved set. [Marchionini (2006)](https://doi.org/10.1145/1121949.1121979) partitions the regime: *lookup* (fact retrieval, known-item, the well-posed IR/state-space case where T is fixed) versus *exploratory* search (learn/investigate, where T is endogenous and under construction). The exploratory regime is where T itself changes during the episode, breaking the assumption that U(·, T) has a fixed argument.

### 4.3 Scent: where human boundedness lives

The central perceptual constraint is **information scent**: the searcher's imperfect estimate of a distal source's value and cost, computed from *proximal* cues (link text, snippets, titles, citations) ([Pirolli and Card, 1999](https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-295X.106.4.643); [Pirolli, 2007](https://doi.org/10.1093/acprof:oso/9780195173321.001.0001)). Foraging decisions (which link to follow, whether to continue) are made on scent, *not on content*, so the quality of human search is bounded by how well proximal cues predict distal value. In P, the human's estimate of rel(d, T) at decision time is a noisy function of a surrogate, not of d itself. This is corroborated empirically: document surrogates and query-biased snippets strongly govern human relevance judgments, and needs are frequently satisfied from the surrogate alone without opening the document ([Hearst, 2009](https://searchuserinterfaces.com/book/sui_ch5_retrieval_results.html)).

### 4.4 Affective dynamics

Human search carries a state variable with no obvious machine analogue. [Kuhlthau's (1991)](https://doi.org/10.1002/(SICI)1097-4571(199106)42:5%3C361::AID-ASI6%3E3.0.CO;2-%23) Information Search Process describes six stages (initiation, selection, exploration, formulation, collection, presentation), each with cognitive, affective, and physical realms, governed by an Uncertainty Principle: uncertainty and anxiety are highest early and often *increase* as initial information is encountered, before a focus forms, then fall. Search dynamics are thus partly driven by affect, not solely by relevance, a determinant of when a person stalls or quits.

### 4.5 Action primitives and scope

[Ellis's (1989)](https://doi.org/10.1108/eb026843) six behavioral features give a small set of composable, non-sequential primitives for human seeking: starting, chaining (following citations/links), browsing, differentiating (filtering by quality), monitoring, and extracting. These map almost one-to-one onto agent tool operations (§5), giving a shared action vocabulary for the contrast. [Wilson's (1999)](https://doi.org/10.1108/EUM0000000007145) nested model locates the level we formalize: information behavior ⊃ information-seeking behavior ⊃ information *search* behavior (the innermost, system-interaction level), and reminds us that much human acquisition is passive (active/ongoing/passive seeking, passive attention) rather than query-driven, a mode current agents largely lack.

### 4.6 The human stopping rule

Two stopping characterizations apply. The optimal one is Charnov's Marginal Value Theorem, which IFT imports: leave the current patch when its instantaneous within-patch gain rate g′(t_W) falls to the average environmental rate R, i.e. g′(t*) = R at the optimal residence time ([Charnov, 1976](https://doi.org/10.1016/0040-5809(76)90040-X); [Pirolli and Card, 1999](https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-295X.106.4.643)). As between-patch cost falls (fast search engines), optimal time-per-patch falls, so people visit more, shallower patches. The *descriptive* one is satisficing: a boundedly rational searcher sets an aspiration level A and stops at the first option with U ≥ A rather than maximizing ([Simon, 1955](https://doi.org/10.2307/1884852)). Empirically, people stop when they feel able to complete the task even when more or better information is believed available ([Prabha et al., 2007](https://doi.org/10.1108/00220410710723894)). The human σ is therefore an aspiration/marginal-rate rule under time and attention cost, not an explicit utility-versus-budget computation.

At the micro level this is machine-observable. The cascade click model formalizes SERP behavior: under the examination hypothesis, P(click i) = P(examine i)·P(relevant i); in the cascade model the user scans strictly top-to-bottom, clicks result i with probability equal to its relevance r_i, and *stops after the first click*, giving P(click i) = r_i · Π_{j<i}(1 − r_j) ([Craswell et al., 2008](https://doi.org/10.1145/1341531.1341545); survey in [Chuklin, Markov and de Rijke, 2015](https://doi.org/10.2200/S00654ED1V01Y201507ICR043)). This is scent-following plus a satisficing stop rendered as a generative model, and it is exactly why the PRP's top-down assumption is the right ordering for human consumption. Query reformulation between actions is likewise a measurable edit space (add/remove/substitute/reorder words, spelling and acronym fixes, URL stripping, sub/superstring) with effectiveness read from click patterns ([Huang and Efthimiadis, 2009](https://doi.org/10.1145/1645953.1645966)), operationalizing berrypicking as a concrete move set.

## 5. The agent searcher

### 5.1 The rational-agent / metareasoning foundation

The normative standard for any non-omniscient searcher is not perfect rationality but bounded optimality. [Russell and Subramanian (1995)](https://doi.org/10.1613/jair.133) define an agent program p as bounded-optimal if, for a fixed machine M and environment E, p maximizes expected utility of the behavior produced by running p on M in E: optimality over *realizable programs on a fixed machine*, not over actions assuming infinite compute. [Russell (1997)](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0004-3702(97)00026-X) places this against perfect, calculative, and metalevel rationality and argues bounded optimality is the right foundation.

The stopping rule σ for such an agent is supplied by rational metareasoning. Treat each candidate computation (a deliberation, retrieval, or tool step) S_j as a meta-level action with

```
VOC(S_j) = E[ utility of the object-level action chosen after running S_j ] − E[ utility of acting now ] − Cost(S_j),
```

and run the computation of highest positive net value, committing (stopping) when no available computation has positive net VOC ([Russell and Wefald, 1991](https://doi.org/10.1016/0004-3702(91)90015-C)). [Horvitz (1987](https://arxiv.org/abs/1304.2759), [1988)](https://aaai.org/papers/00111-aaai88-020-reasoning-under-varying-and-uncertain-resource-constraints/) developed the same stop/continue tradeoff as expected value of computation with flexible/anytime procedures whose partial results improve monotonically, the operational pattern behind iterative, streaming agent search. This is the meta-level twin of the economic reservation-value rule (§2.3): keep searching iff the expected marginal value of the next step exceeds its cost.

### 5.2 The retrieve step as a learned, differentiable relevance function

Modern agents inherit the §3 retrieve/read decomposition but replace fixed scoring with learned components. Dense passage retrieval represents query and passage with separate encoders and scores by inner product sim(q,p) = E_Q(q)·E_P(p), retrieving the top-K by maximum inner-product search over a precomputed index, with encoders trained contrastively ([Karpukhin et al., 2020](https://aclanthology.org/2020.emnlp-main.550/)). This makes rel(d, T) a *learned, optimizable* function in a vector space rather than the fixed lexical statistic of BM25.

Retrieval-Augmented Generation makes the whole retrieve-then-read step a single probabilistic object. Given input x, a retriever p_η(z | x) returns top-K passages and a generator p_θ(y | x, z) produces the answer, marginalizing over retrieved evidence (per output for RAG-Sequence, per token for RAG-Token) with retriever and generator trained jointly ([Lewis et al., 2020](https://arxiv.org/abs/2005.11401)). Here relevance is a *latent variable z*, differentiable through the marginalization: the IR ranking function becomes a learned component of an end-to-end objective, the cleanest bridge from §2.4 to a decision-theoretic answer objective. Precursors and variants instantiate the same idea differently: REALM learns the retriever as a latent variable from the masked-LM signal ([Guu et al., 2020](https://arxiv.org/abs/2002.08909)); RETRO conditions on frozen kNN-retrieved chunks via chunked cross-attention over a ~2-trillion-token database, matching much larger parametric models ([Borgeaud et al., 2022](https://arxiv.org/abs/2112.04426)); Atlas jointly trains a Contriever retriever with a Fusion-in-Decoder reader for sample-efficient few-shot search ([Izacard et al., 2022](https://arxiv.org/abs/2208.03299)); and the Differentiable Search Index collapses index and retriever into one Transformer that maps a query directly to a document identifier, reframing retrieval as autoregressive generation ([Tay et al., 2022](https://arxiv.org/abs/2202.06991)). The parametric-versus-non-parametric memory split these works expose is the agent-specific analogue of "internal knowledge versus external corpus."

### 5.3 The agent as a sequential search policy

Single-shot RAG is one object-level action. Agentic search lifts it to a multi-step policy. ReAct defines an LLM policy that interleaves a free-form reasoning trace (thought_t) with discrete text actions (action_t ∈ {search[·], lookup[·], finish[·]}) executed against an environment, receiving observation_t each step ([Yao et al., 2023](https://arxiv.org/abs/2210.03629)). This is exactly P's object level instantiated with language actions over a partially observable belief state: π(action_t | context_t) is a policy, and the reasoning trace maintains and revises the plan. WebGPT instantiated A in a real browser via a fixed command interface (search, click, scroll, quote, navigate, end) with mandatory citation collection, trained by imitation plus RLHF ([Nakano et al., 2021](https://arxiv.org/abs/2112.09332)), the direct ancestor of deep-research agents. Toolformer makes search one tool-call among many, with the model learning when and how to call APIs ([Schick et al., 2023](https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.04761)).

Multi-hop search is formalized as interleaving retrieval and reasoning. IRCoT uses each chain-of-thought step as a query and conditions the next step on the retrieved documents, so reasoning guides retrieval and vice versa ([Trivedi et al., 2023](https://aclanthology.org/2023.acl-long.557/)); Self-Ask makes query decomposition into explicit follow-up sub-questions a first-class operator ([Press et al., 2023](https://arxiv.org/abs/2210.03350)). These render Ellis's chaining and Bates's berrypicking as explicit, repeated operators in π.

### 5.4 The agent's stopping rule and objective

Where the human σ is implicit satisficing, the agent's σ can be made explicit. FLARE renders the *when-to-search* decision as a runtime control signal: provisionally generate the next sentence and retrieve iff the minimum token confidence falls below a threshold τ, otherwise continue ([Jiang et al., 2023](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.06983)), a confidence-gated instance of the value-of-computation stopping rule. Self-RAG internalizes both the relevance judge and the stop/continue decision via trained reflection tokens that decide on-demand retrieval and critique generations for relevance, support, and utility ([Asai et al., 2023](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.11511)), folding the IR evaluation step into the searcher itself.

The objective J can also be made literal and trainable. Search-R1 trains the LLM with reinforcement learning to interleave reasoning and real search-engine calls, masking retrieved tokens from the policy-gradient loss and optimizing a simple outcome reward (answer correctness) over multi-turn trajectories ([Jin et al., 2025](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.09516)). This is the decision-theoretic search objective of §2.1 made concrete: expected-reward maximization over search trajectories, with query count and stopping emerging from optimization rather than from a hand-set heuristic.

A critical caveat for the formalism: the agent's search objective is *not single-valued across methods*. RAG optimizes likelihood marginalization; WebGPT and Self-Ask largely imitate human trajectories; Search-R1 maximizes an outcome reward; FLARE/Self-RAG optimize a confidence/sufficiency signal. "The agent's objective" must therefore be stated per method, not assumed uniform, a survey framing in [Singh, Ehtesham, Kumar, Talaei Khoei and Vasilakos (2025)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.09136) and [Xi et al. (2025)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.05668).

### 5.5 The agent's cost model

The agent's c is denominated in tokens, compute (FLOPs/GPU-time), latency, and money, not in attention or anxiety. Crucially, the agent can *parallelize* (fan out many queries at once), *read full text* cheaply rather than skim surrogates, *persist* across many hops without fatigue, and *iterate* reformulations far past a human's satisficing point. Benchmarks built to stress these capabilities, multi-hop QA (HotpotQA, 2WikiMultihopQA, MuSiQue) and persistent web navigation (GAIA, BrowseComp), show both the agent advantage and the remaining human gap: on [GAIA](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.12983), humans score ~92% versus ~15% for a strong contemporaneous tool-using model ([Mialon et al., 2023](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.12983)); [BrowseComp](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.12516)'s 1,266 short-verifiable-answer questions require persistent multi-hop navigation that non-browsing models essentially cannot do, while browsing-trained agents do markedly better ([Wei et al., 2025](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.12516); headline accuracy figures circulating in third-party reporting should be checked against the source).

## 6. How human and agent search differ, formally

The §2 frame lets us state the contrast as differences in specific components of P rather than as a clash of paradigms. The economics-of-interaction view ([Azzopardi, 2014](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/2600428.2609574)) casts an interactive session as choosing interaction variables (queries issued, documents assessed) to maximize information gain minus interaction cost, where the cost and benefit terms are *slotted differently* by searcher type. This is what makes one parameterized problem out of two.

| Axis (component of P) | Human searcher | Agent searcher | Difference, formally |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Objective J** | Rate of information gain R = G/(T_B+T_W) under satisficing; implicit, situational ([Pirolli and Card, 1999](https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-295X.106.4.643); [Simon, 1955](https://doi.org/10.2307/1884852)) | Explicit, often trainable: likelihood marginalization, imitation, or outcome-reward RL ([Lewis et al., 2020](https://arxiv.org/abs/2005.11401); [Jin et al., 2025](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.09516)) | U is implicit/aspiration-based for the human, explicit/optimized for the agent; agent J is method-dependent |
| **Cost model c** | Time, attention, cognitive load, affect ([Kuhlthau, 1991](https://doi.org/10.1002/(SICI)1097-4571(199106)42:5%3C361::AID-ASI6%3E3.0.CO;2-%23)) | Tokens, compute, latency, money ([Yao et al., 2023](https://arxiv.org/abs/2210.03629)) | Same c-minus-benefit objective ([Azzopardi, 2014](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/2600428.2609574)), different denomination, so different optimum and stopping point |
| **Action/query space A** | Few queries; reformulation as small edits; serial patch-hopping ([Huang and Efthimiadis, 2009](https://doi.org/10.1145/1645953.1645966); [Ellis, 1989](https://doi.org/10.1108/eb026843)) | Many queries, decomposition, tool calls, navigation; reformulation as an explicit operator ([Press et al., 2023](https://arxiv.org/abs/2210.03350); [Schick et al., 2023](https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.04761)) | A is far larger and cheaper-per-action for the agent; same primitives (chain/browse/differentiate/extract) but vastly higher cardinality |
| **Perception (estimate of rel)** | Estimates rel(d,T) from surrogates/snippets (scent); often answers from the surrogate alone ([Pirolli and Card, 1999](https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-295X.106.4.643); [Hearst, 2009](https://searchuserinterfaces.com/book/sui_ch5_retrieval_results.html)) | Reads full passages; rel is a learned function over content ([Karpukhin et al., 2020](https://aclanthology.org/2020.emnlp-main.550/); [Lewis et al., 2020](https://arxiv.org/abs/2005.11401)) | Human rel-estimate is a noisy surrogate function; agent rel-estimate is over distal content, so scent's gating role collapses |
| **Serial vs parallel** | Strictly serial, top-down scan ([Craswell et al., 2008](https://doi.org/10.1145/1341531.1341545)) | Parallel fan-out, persistent multi-hop ([Wei et al., 2025](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.12516)) | The trajectory in P is a single chain for the human, a tree/DAG for the agent |
| **Relevance signals (stratum)** | Situational, cognitive, affective relevance ([Saracevic, 2007](https://doi.org/10.1002/asi.20682); [Mizzaro, 1997](https://doi.org/10.1002/(SICI)1097-4571(199709)48:9%3C810::AID-ASI6%3E3.0.CO;2-U)) | System/topical and answer-verifiable relevance ([Robertson, 1977](https://doi.org/10.1108/eb026647); [Jin et al., 2025](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.09516)) | Human and agent optimize *different strata* of rel(d,T); divergence here is the core mismatch |
| **Stopping rule σ** | Satisficing aspiration / marginal-value rule ([Simon, 1955](https://doi.org/10.2307/1884852); [Charnov, 1976](https://doi.org/10.1016/0040-5809(76)90040-X); [Prabha et al., 2007](https://doi.org/10.1108/00220410710723894)) | VOC/confidence/budget/reward-termination ([Russell and Wefald, 1991](https://doi.org/10.1016/0004-3702(91)90015-C); [Jiang et al., 2023](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.06983)) | Both are marginal-value rules; the human's threshold is implicit and affect-influenced, the agent's is explicit and tunable |
| **Evaluation** | Cranfield precision/recall + user studies of effort, preference, trust ([Cleverdon, 1967](https://doi.org/10.1108/eb050097); [Kaiser et al., 2025](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3706599.3720123)) | Answer-verifiable agent benchmarks ([Mialon et al., 2023](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.12983); [Wei et al., 2025](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.12516)) | Evaluation targets the ranking/effort for humans, the terminal answer for agents; the user's own evaluation loop is collapsed |

Two empirical results sharpen the contrast and warn against treating the agent as a strict improvement. A large tracked study (1,526 US participants) found generative-AI search users were faster and more often correct than traditional-search users, yet subjectively preferred traditional search, with preference depending on personality ([Kaiser et al., 2025](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3706599.3720123)). A randomized experiment (~12,000 queries, ~80,000 results, seven countries) found baseline trust in generative search lower than traditional, but that citations raise trust *even when hallucinated*, that revealing model uncertainty backfires, and that higher trust leads to more clicking and *less* evaluation time ([Li and Aral, 2025](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.06435)). In P terms: when the searcher delegates to an agent that returns a synthesized answer rather than a ranked list, the human's stopping rule σ and relevance check are *removed from the loop* and replaced by the agent's, and the human's residual trust signal (citations) can be gamed, with a corresponding supply-side response in generative-engine optimization ([Aggarwal et al., 2024](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.09735)).

## 7. Open problems and implications

**Evaluation for agentic search.** Cranfield/TREC scores a ranking against fixed relevance judgments ([Cleverdon, 1967](https://doi.org/10.1108/eb050097); [Voorhees and Harman, 2005](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262220736/trec/)), but an agent emits a synthesized, cited answer, not a list. Answer-verifiable benchmarks ([Mialon et al., 2023](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.12983); [Wei et al., 2025](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.12516)) address final-answer correctness but not the quality of the *process* (faithfulness to evidence, calibration, cost-efficiency of the trajectory). A unified evaluation should score J(π, σ) directly: terminal answer value minus realized search cost, across both searcher types, which neither paradigm currently does.

**Optimal stopping for agents.** The classical reservation-value and VOC rules ([Weitzman, 1979](https://doi.org/10.2307/1912769); [Russell and Wefald, 1991](https://doi.org/10.1016/0004-3702(91)90015-C)) assume a calibrated estimate of the marginal value of continuing. LLM agents are poorly calibrated, and current σ implementations (confidence gates ([Jiang et al., 2023](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.06983)), reflection tokens ([Asai et al., 2023](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.11511)), fixed step budgets) are heuristics. Deriving a properly bounded-optimal σ ([Russell and Subramanian, 1995](https://doi.org/10.1613/jair.133)) for a token-and-money cost model, with calibrated value-of-information estimates, is open.

**Shared versus divergent objectives.** The §6 table shows human and agent optimize different relevance strata. When a person delegates search to an agent, the agent optimizes system/topical/answer-verifiable relevance while the human cares about situational/cognitive relevance ([Saracevic, 2007](https://doi.org/10.1002/asi.20682)). Aligning the agent's J with the human's true T, given that T is anomalous and evolving ([Belkin et al., 1982](https://doi.org/10.1108/eb026722); [Bates, 1989](https://doi.org/10.1108/eb024320)), is the search-specific instance of the alignment problem, made worse by the trust dynamics that suppress human re-evaluation ([Li and Aral, 2025](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.06435)).

**Privacy when the searcher is an agent.** Privacy-preserving search infrastructure (IP anonymization, proxying queries to a search engine) was designed around the human cost model: a person issues a handful of queries, and unlinking those queries from an identity protects a low-rate, human-paced signal. An agent searcher inverts every assumption that made this adequate. The agent's action space A is far larger and its per-query cost negligible (§5.5), so it fans out *many* queries per task in parallel; the resulting query stream is a dense, machine-paced trace whose *content and timing* leak the underlying need far more than any single IP would. Anonymizing the network identity does not anonymize the *search trajectory*: the sequence of reformulations and follow-ups (the agent's externalized berrypicking, [Bates, 1989](https://doi.org/10.1108/eb024320)) is itself identifying and need-revealing, and an agent optimizing an explicit outcome reward ([Jin et al., 2025](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.09516)) has no incentive to minimize query volume or obfuscate its trace unless that cost is priced into c. Two implications follow. First, IP-level anonymization is necessary but not sufficient for agent searchers; the privacy unit should be the *trajectory*, not the packet. Second, because the agent can cheaply parallelize and persist, it should be possible to *budget* queries against a privacy cost added to c, making "search less revealingly" a term in the agent's objective J, which has no clean analogue in the human model where query volume was naturally bounded by attention. This is a concrete instance of the §6 thesis: re-parameterizing the cost model c by searcher type changes not just the optimum but the threat model.

The throughline is that "search" is one problem, J(π, σ) = E[U(b_τ, T) − Σ c], with the searcher as a parameter. The human and the agent differ in how they fill in U, c, A, π, and σ, and almost every practically important difference, from snippet-skimming to trust to privacy, is a difference in *one* of those components, statable in the single frame rather than across three incommensurable ones.

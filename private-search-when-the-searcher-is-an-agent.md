# Private search when the searcher is an agent

This report bridges two earlier pieces of the corpus: the catalog of IP-anonymization and private-search mechanisms (Tor, mixnets, Oblivious HTTP, Private Information Retrieval, query obfuscation, and the attack side, including website fingerprinting and the AOL deanonymization) and the formalization of the search problem as a single decision-theoretic tuple. The earlier formalization closed by asking what privacy-preserving search infrastructure implies once the searcher is an agent rather than a person. This is the answer.

The thesis has two parts. First, privacy is not external to the search problem. It belongs inside the agent's objective `J`, either as a cost term that is paid alongside tokens and latency, or as a constraint on the policy that issues queries. Second, the agent's cost profile differs from the human's along exactly the axes that determine which privacy mechanisms are viable. A human searcher pays primarily in attention and is intolerant of latency; an agent pays in tokens, compute, and money, and on a background task tolerates seconds to minutes of delay and generates queries essentially for free. That single substitution in the cost vector flips the verdict on most of the mechanisms the corpus catalogs. Things that were research curiosities for interactive humans (deliberate per-hop mixing delay, heavy server-side PIR compute, constant-rate cover traffic) become deployable defaults for a patient agent. At the same time, the agent's denser, machine-regular, intent-revealing query trajectory makes some threats strictly worse than they were for a person.

We build on the prior frame and do not restate the whole corpus. We assume the search-episode tuple `P = < B, b0, T, A, RESULT, U, c, π, σ >` and the objective `J(π, σ) = E[ U(b_τ, T) − Σ c ]` from the formalization, and we assume the mechanism and attack catalog from the IP-anonymization report.

## 1. Privacy as a term in J

In the base formalization the searcher chooses a policy `π` (object level: which query to issue, which document to read) and a stopping rule `σ` (meta level: stop and answer?) to maximize expected terminal value minus accumulated search cost:

```
J(π, σ) = E [ U(b_τ, T) − Σ_{t<τ} c(b_{t−1}, a_t) ].
```

Privacy enters because each external action a search episode takes is observed by parties other than the searcher. Issuing a query exposes it to the search engine; reading a document exposes the fetch to the destination, to on-path networks, and (under fingerprinting) to anyone watching the encrypted channel. The episode therefore emits an *observable trace* `O(τ)`: the ordered set of `(query, timing, endpoint, fetched-object)` tuples visible to one or more adversaries. The base objective silently assumes this trace is free. It is not.

There are two clean ways to put privacy into `J`.

**(a) Privacy as a leakage cost added to `c`.** Augment the per-action cost with a leakage term, so the searcher optimizes

```
J'(π, σ) = E [ U(b_τ, T) − Σ_t ( c(b_{t−1}, a_t) + λ · ℓ(a_t, O_{<t}) ) ].
```

Here `ℓ(a_t, O_{<t})` is the *marginal information an adversary gains* about the searcher's identity or intent `T` from action `a_t`, given everything already observed, and `λ ≥ 0` is the price the principal places on a unit of leakage. The conditioning on `O_{<t}` is essential and is where the agent story diverges from the human one: leakage is not a property of a single query but of the *sequence*, because each sub-query narrows the adversary's posterior over `T`. The natural instantiation of `ℓ` is the reduction in the adversary's uncertainty, for example the drop in entropy of the posterior over the searcher's identity within an anonymity set, or the mutual information `I(O ; T)` between the trace and the true intent. The privacy mechanisms in the corpus are precisely interventions that lower `ℓ`: onion routing and OHTTP lower the identity-linkage component, PIR drives the query-content component toward zero, and cover traffic raises the adversary's conditional entropy.

**(b) Privacy as a constraint on `π`.** Alternatively, treat privacy as a hard requirement and optimize utility subject to an *unlinkability constraint* on the query stream:

```
maximize J(π, σ)  subject to  I(O(τ) ; ID) ≤ ε   and/or   A(q) ≥ k for all emitted q,
```

where `ID` is the searcher's identity, `ε` bounds how much the trace may reveal about it, and `A(q) ≥ k` requires that every emitted query be indistinguishable among at least `k` plausible originators (an anonymity-set / k-anonymity constraint on the live stream rather than on a static log). This is the formulation that matches a security requirement ("no single endpoint may link two of my queries") rather than a tunable preference.

Two refinements matter for agents specifically.

The first is **cumulative, not per-query, accounting.** A human issues a sparse, self-censored handful of queries per session. An agent issues a long-lived, correlated stream. Bounding leakage per query is insufficient when the join of many low-leakage queries is high-leakage. The right object is a privacy budget spent over the whole stream, which is exactly the model of differential privacy *under continual observation* ([Dwork et al., 2010](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/1806689.1806787)): a streaming release is private only if cumulative loss is bounded over the entire sequence, not at each step. The agent's `J'` should carry a running budget, and repeated identical queries should be answered from a memoized noisy result rather than re-leaked, the trick [RAPPOR (Erlingsson et al., 2014)](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/2660267.2660348) uses to stop averaging-out attacks under repeated observation.

The second is that `λ` (or `ε`) is a *property of the principal, not the searcher*. The same agent run for an investigative journalist and for a casual fact-check faces different privacy prices. Making privacy a term in `J` rather than a fixed network setting is what lets an agent trade it against cost and quality the way it already trades latency against thoroughness. That tradeoff is the whole point of the rest of this report.

## 2. Why an agent's privacy exposure differs from a human's

The agent is worse on two axes and better on two others. Both directions follow from the same root cause: the agent is a program, not a person.

**Worse: volume and machine-regularity.** A deep-research agent does not issue one query; it decomposes an information need `T` into many sub-queries and chains them. The log study [Ning et al. (2026), *Agentic Search in the Wild*](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.17617), labeling 14.44M real agentic search requests over 3.97M sessions sourced via the DeepResearchGym API, documents that agents iteratively reformulate and chain queries as a matter of course. Each step is another observation in `O(τ)`. The trajectory is a denser exposure surface than a human's sparse query stream, and it is emitted at machine cadence: concurrent, low-jitter bursts with none of the reading pauses, mouse telemetry, or self-censoring hesitation that characterize a human session. That regularity is exactly the signal anti-bot systems key on. [Cloudflare (2025), *Moving past bots vs. humans*](https://blog.cloudflare.com/past-bots-and-humans/) reports that AI-crawler traffic is now a measurable and growing share of requests (GPTBot rising from roughly 4.7% to 11.7% of AI-bot traffic and Meta-ExternalAgent from roughly 8.5% to 11.6% between July 2024 and July 2025) and that AI bots are fingerprinted by signature and blocked. The metadata-layer adversary the corpus described, website-fingerprinting attacks that recover the visited site from encrypted traffic shape, gets a *cleaner* trace from an agent. The attack lineage ([Panchenko et al., 2011](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/2046556.2046570); [Wang et al., 2014](https://www.usenix.org/conference/usenixsecurity14/technical-sessions/presentation/wang_tao); [Sirinam et al., 2018](https://arxiv.org/abs/1801.02265), the last reaching over 90% accuracy against defended Tor traffic) was built against human dwell-time jitter as incidental cover. Remove the jitter and the classifier's job is easier.

**Worse: a richer linkable fingerprint and intent inversion.** The content-layer threat the corpus anchored on the AOL release is sharper for agents. The existence proof remains [Barbaro and Zeller (2006)](https://www.nytimes.com/2006/08/09/technology/09aol.html): searcher 4417749 was re-identified as Thelma Arnold from query content alone, proving that a user's bag of query terms is a quasi-identifier and that stripping IP and username does not anonymize a log. With agent-scale logs the quasi-identifier vector is larger, because the agent emits verbatim slices of its prompt and chains self-referential sub-queries that a self-conscious human would never type in sequence. Worse, the *order and decomposition themselves* leak. [Yao et al. (2025), *IntentMiner*](https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.14166) (an arXiv preprint, not peer-reviewed; authors Yunhao Yao, Zhiqiang Wang, Haoran Cheng, Yihang Cheng, Haohua Du, Xiang-Yang Li) reconstructs a user's sensitive intent from a semi-honest server's view of step-level MCP tool-call logs, *without the original prompt*, reporting roughly 85% semantic alignment with the original intent on the ToolACE benchmark. Functional decomposition forces semantic transparency: a sub-query for blood-pressure norms and another for low-sodium recipes jointly triangulate a sensitive medical condition that no single query stated. This is the agent-native analogue of AOL query-log linkage, but it operates on the live reformulation chain rather than a static log, which means a per-query defense that scrubs any single query fails when the *sequence* still leaks. The same multi-step transparency is what makes the cumulative-budget accounting of §1 mandatory rather than optional.

There is also a class of leakage with no human analogue at all: the shared LLM infrastructure behind the agent memorizes and can exfiltrate ([Carlini et al., 2021](https://arxiv.org/abs/2012.07805) extract verbatim PII from a language model via black-box queries), and agents conflate trusted instructions with untrusted tool output, leaking contextually-private information they should withhold ([Mireshghallah et al., 2024](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.17884), whose ConfAIde benchmark shows GPT-4 revealing contextually-private information 39% of the time; [Ukani et al., 2025](https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.07725), a measurement of eight browser agents). These are real, but they are leakage *into and out of the model*, orthogonal to the transport-and-query-stream problem this report scopes. We flag them and move on.

**Better: programmatic control over timing and batching.** Everything a human does on the network carries involuntary human timing. An agent's timing is a free variable. It can delay, batch, reorder, and rate-limit its own queries to whatever schedule a privacy mechanism prefers, because there is no impatient person watching a spinner. This is the precondition for the latency-tolerance flip in §3, and it directly relaxes the constraint formulation in §1: the agent can *choose* an emission schedule that satisfies an unlinkability constraint, where a human cannot reschedule their own curiosity.

**Better: cover traffic is nearly free.** A human cannot be asked to type plausible decoy queries all day; doing so was the central usability failure of decoy-injection tools. An agent generates contextually plausible, semantically diverse decoy queries at scale for the marginal cost of a few tokens, and can sustain them at a constant rate indefinitely. In the `J'` framing, the leakage term `ℓ` can be driven down by raising the adversary's conditional entropy, and the cost of doing so (`c` for decoy generation) is, for an agent, close to zero. For a human it was prohibitive.

The net picture: a human's exposure is a sparse, human-paced, content-based query stream. An agent's is a dense, machine-regular, parallel trajectory that reconstructs intent at higher fidelity and is trivially bot-classified, but is also fully under programmatic control and can afford defenses a human never could. Sections 3 and 4 cash out that asymmetry.

## 3. The latency-tolerance flip

This is the core insight. The privacy mechanisms in the corpus were repeatedly judged "too slow" or "too annoying" for interactive search. That judgment was made against a human's cost function, in which latency is paid as attention and decoy queries are paid as irritation. Re-price the same mechanisms against a patient background agent and the verdict inverts.

**Mixnet delay is a tunable knob, not a tax.** Loopix ([Piotrowska et al., 2017](https://www.usenix.org/conference/usenixsecurity17/technical-sessions/presentation/piotrowska)) and its production descendant Nym add per-hop Poisson mixing delay by design: each node independently holds each packet for an exponentially distributed time, so that input and output cannot be linked even by a global passive adversary. The processing overhead is small (under 1.5 ms per hop, over 300 messages per second per node), but the *security delay* is deliberate and lands end-to-end in the range of hundreds of milliseconds to several seconds (a Nym-style instantiation with roughly 50 ms mean delay per hop over three hops gives 500 to 800 ms before the tail). For interactive human use this is the failure point. [Davitt, Ristea, and Murdoch (2026)](https://petsymposium.org/popets/2026/popets-2026-0029.php), a peer-reviewed PoPETs paper, empirically bound human tolerance for mixnet-mediated real-time interaction: usability holds at 1 to 4 seconds of mean delay, shows difficulty at around 7 seconds, and becomes detrimental, breaking collaboration, at around 10 seconds. A background agent is not real-time. It has no 7-second cliff. The mean-delay knob `1/μ` that buys better anonymity at the cost of latency can simply be turned up, because the agent pays no attention cost for waiting. The very parameter that makes mixnets unusable for human chat is free for an agent, which converts mixnet delay from a usability blocker into an anonymity dial.

**PIR compute is amortizable, not prohibitive.** Private Information Retrieval lets a client retrieve from a server without revealing which item it wanted, which is the strongest possible answer to the query-content leakage of §2: the search engine returns a result without learning the query. The corpus catalogs the cost as the obstacle. [Tiptoe (Henzinger et al., 2023)](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3600006.3613134) does cryptography-only private nearest-neighbor search over 360M web pages at 145 core-seconds of server compute, 2.7 seconds of online latency, and 56.9 MiB of client-server communication, of which roughly 74% sits in a *query-independent offline phase*. For an interactive human, 2.7 seconds plus tens of megabytes per query plus a heavy server bill is a non-starter. For an agent, the offline phase is pre-fetchable during idle time, the multi-second online latency is off the interactive critical path entirely, and the bandwidth is a line item the principal can price into `λ`. Private RAG inherits the same logic: [PIR-RAG (Wang et al., 2025)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.21325) (an unrefereed preprint; treat its numbers as author-reported) reports roughly 16.8 seconds end-to-end on MS MARCO, including document fetch, by batching retrieval and content-fetch into one lattice-PIR query, against a Tiptoe-style baseline they put near 24 seconds. Tens of seconds for a private retrieval is a deal-breaker for a person and an entirely ordinary step latency for a deep-research agent that already spends minutes on a task. Where the corpus to be searched must *also* stay private, [PRAG (Zyskind, South, and Pentland, 2023/2024)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.12955) (peer-reviewed at the PrivateNLP workshop at ACL 2024) gives an MPC route that hides both the query vector and the database with sublinear communication.

**Cover traffic is free instead of annoying.** Query-obfuscation tools inject decoys to break the link between a profile and a true intent: TrackMeNot sends periodic fakes over time, GooPIR co-submits k−1 fakes with each real query so the server sees k indistinguishable candidates ([Howe and Nissenbaum, 2009](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1109.4677)). For humans this was weak. [Peddinti and Saxena (2010)](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-642-14527-8_2) showed that an off-the-shelf ML classifier re-separates real queries from TrackMeNot decoys given even a short query history, because human-realistic decoy generation is hard and the real queries cluster. An agent inverts both weaknesses. It generates contextually plausible, semantically diverse decoys at scale, raising the classifier's separation cost, and it can sustain constant-rate cover traffic continuously, which is exactly the steady dummy traffic that defeats the statistical-disclosure and long-term intersection attacks that single-shot decoys could not. The thing that annoyed humans into uninstalling TrackMeNot costs an agent a few tokens.

The throughline: latency-tolerance and free query generation are the two agent properties that move mixnets, PIR, and cover traffic from the "research curiosity" column to the "deployable default" column. The flip is not that the mechanisms got cheaper; it is that the agent's cost function stopped charging for the resource they consume.

## 4. Re-evaluating each mechanism for an agent searcher

The table below re-scores the corpus's mechanism families against the two cost functions. "Human viability" is the corpus's prior verdict for interactive human search; "Agent viability" is the re-priced verdict for a patient background agent. The shift column names the agent property doing the work.

| Mechanism | Human viability | Agent viability | What flips it |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Tor / onion routing** | Moderate. Works but slow and heavily blocked; Tor exit IPs are a public, auto-blockable list and anti-bot layers give them poor reputation. | Moderate-to-low for fetching, but for a *different* reason. Latency is fine for an agent, but the agent's machine-regular traffic is *more* fingerprintable ([Sirinam et al., 2018](https://arxiv.org/abs/1801.02265)) and exit-blocking still bites. Useful where destination anonymity matters and the target does not block Tor. | Latency tolerance helps; traffic regularity and exit-blocking hurt. Net wash. |
| **Mixnets (Loopix / Nym)** | Low. Per-hop mixing delay (seconds) breaks past the ~7–10s human tolerance ceiling ([Davitt et al., 2026](https://petsymposium.org/popets/2026/popets-2026-0029.php)). | High. The delay knob `1/μ` can be turned *up* for stronger anonymity since the agent pays no attention cost ([Piotrowska et al., 2017](https://www.usenix.org/conference/usenixsecurity17/technical-sessions/presentation/piotrowska)). Strongest defense against global passive metadata adversaries. | Latency tolerance. The agent is not real-time, so the security delay is free. |
| **OHTTP / ODoH** | High. Single relay hop, near-zero added latency; already production-deployed. | Highest / cheapest win. Fits agent-to-API and agent-to-tool calls exactly: the relay sees IP not content, the gateway sees content not IP, and repeated requests are mutually uncorrelatable ([RFC 9458, Thomson and Wood, 2024](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc9458.html)). Deployable today. | No flip needed; it was already cheap. Agents should make it the default transport for tool traffic. |
| **PIR (private RAG / private vector search)** | Low. Multi-second latency, tens of MiB per query, heavy server compute are unacceptable interactively. | High. Offline phase is pre-fetchable; online latency is off the critical path; bandwidth is a priced line item ([Tiptoe, Henzinger et al., 2023](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3600006.3613134); [PIR-RAG, Wang et al., 2025](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.21325); [PRAG, Zyskind et al., 2023/2024](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.12955)). Drives query-content leakage `ℓ` toward zero. | Latency tolerance plus compute budget. Tens of seconds is an ordinary agent step. |
| **Query obfuscation (TrackMeNot / GooPIR)** | Low. Decoys are detectable by ML classifiers given short history; human-realistic decoy generation is hard and annoying ([Peddinti and Saxena, 2010](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-642-14527-8_2)). | Moderate-to-high. Agents generate diverse, plausible decoys for free at constant rate, raising separation cost and supplying the steady cover that defeats intersection attacks. Weaker than PIR (it raises entropy rather than zeroing leakage) but composes with everything. | Free query generation. The agent absorbs the cost that made it useless for humans. |
| **Residential proxies** | Low as privacy. Unblocks bot-classified traffic but is a trust-shift, not a privacy gain (see §5). | Low as privacy, same as for humans, and arguably worse because the agent routes a denser, more intent-revealing stream through the proxy operator. Useful only for evasion of bot-blocking, not for anonymity. | Nothing flips. The trust problem is independent of the cost function. |

Two cross-cutting points. First, these mechanisms compose along different layers of the trace `O(τ)`: OHTTP and mixnets attack the identity-linkage component, PIR attacks the query-content component, and obfuscation raises the adversary's conditional entropy over what remains. A serious agent design layers them (for example OHTTP transport plus PIR retrieval plus constant-rate cover) rather than picking one. Second, the cumulative-budget concern from §1 sits underneath all of them: even a PIR-protected query stream can leak through *timing and volume* if the agent does not also regularize its emission schedule, which is why the programmatic-timing advantage of §2 is load-bearing and not a footnote.

## 5. Residential proxies and the agent

The corpus reached a sharp conclusion about residential proxies: they are a *trust-shift, not a privacy gain*. The mechanism by which someone reaches for them is the bot-blocking pressure of §2. An agent's traffic is trivially bot-classified ([Cloudflare, 2025](https://blog.cloudflare.com/past-bots-and-humans/)), so scraping and research agents are pushed toward residential proxy pools to borrow the IP reputation of real consumer connections and get past anti-bot layers. That solves a *delivery* problem (does my request get served?) and does nothing for the *privacy* problem (who learns what I am doing?).

The trust shift is precise. When an agent routes its query stream through a residential proxy operator, it does not remove an observer; it adds one, and it picks the worst possible one. The proxy operator sees the agent's full request stream in the clear at the point of egress, including, for a research agent, the dense intent-revealing trajectory of §2 that is *more* deanonymizing than a human's sparse stream. The operator can log it, profile it, sell it, or be compelled to hand it over. Many residential pools are assembled from consent-questionable sources (SDK-bundled apps, repurposed proxyware), so the operator's incentives and provenance are exactly the ones a privacy-seeking principal should distrust. Where Tor or a mixnet is designed so that no single relay can link identity to content, a residential proxy is designed so that one party (the operator) holds both. It is the inverse of an unlinkability mechanism.

For a scraping agent this means residential proxies should be classified as an *evasion tool, not a privacy tool*, and kept strictly out of the privacy term `ℓ` in `J'`. Using one lowers the bot-block failure rate; it raises identity-linkage leakage by concentrating the whole trace at a single, poorly-incentivized observer. If the goal is to fetch despite blocking, a residential proxy may be the only thing that works, and that is a legitimate operational choice. If the goal is privacy, it is a regression, and pairing it with a real anonymity layer (OHTTP or a mixnet between the agent and the proxy ingress, so the operator sees content but not a stable identity) is the minimum to keep the trust shift from becoming a privacy loss. The agent-specific aggravation is volume: the same denser trajectory that makes the agent easier to deanonymize is the trajectory the proxy operator gets handed in full.

## 6. Open problems

**Private RAG at scale.** Tiptoe, PIR-RAG, and PRAG show private retrieval is *buildable*, but at 360M pages, tens of seconds, and tens of MiB per query the gap to a production web-scale index used by a fleet of agents is large. The offline-phase and bandwidth costs that an individual patient agent can absorb may not amortize cleanly across many agents sharing infrastructure. Open questions: how to share the query-independent offline work across agents without reintroducing linkage; how to keep latency in the "ordinary agent step" range as the corpus grows; and whether the corpus-privacy MPC route (PRAG) can scale beyond workshop-scale benchmarks. The PIR-RAG numbers are author-reported on an unrefereed preprint and need independent replication.

**Optimizing `J` with a privacy term.** Section 1 wrote down `J'` with a leakage cost `ℓ` and a price `λ`, but we do not yet have good estimators for `ℓ` online. Estimating the marginal identity- or intent-leakage of the *next* query given the trace so far is itself an inference problem (it is the adversary's problem, run by the defender), and the intent-inversion results ([Yao et al., 2025](https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.14166)) suggest it is tractable for an attacker and therefore estimable for a defender, but no agent today plans its query sequence to minimize cumulative leakage under a budget. Building a policy `π` that does meta-reasoning over privacy the way it already meta-reasons over cost and confidence is open. So is calibrating `λ` to a principal's actual threat model rather than a guess.

**Unlinkable agent identities and rate-limited anonymous credentials.** The constraint formulation in §1 wants every emitted query to be indistinguishable among `k` originators, but real services rate-limit and authenticate. Anonymous-credential systems such as Privacy Pass let a client prove it is authorized (it solved a challenge, it holds a token) without being linkable across uses, which is the right primitive for an agent that needs to clear bot-blocks and rate limits *without* re-acquiring a stable identity, that is, without the residential-proxy trust shift. How to issue agents rate-limited anonymous credentials that survive at the scale and cadence of agentic search, and how to keep the issuance itself from becoming a linkage point, is open.

**Evaluation.** There is no agreed benchmark for *private* agentic search that scores the three-way tradeoff among utility `U`, cost `c`, and leakage `ℓ` together. The IR tradition has Cranfield/TREC for `U`; the privacy literature has fingerprinting accuracy and intent-reconstruction alignment for `ℓ`; nobody scores them jointly on the same agent trajectories. The log study by [Ning et al. (2026)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.17617) gives realistic agent trajectories, and ConfAIde ([Mireshghallah et al., 2024](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.17884)) and IntentMiner ([Yao et al., 2025](https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.14166)) give leakage probes, but a unified `J'`-shaped evaluation that says "this private-search agent gives up X points of answer quality and Y dollars of compute to cut intent-leakage by Z" does not exist yet. Until it does, the latency-tolerance flip is an argument from mechanism costs, not a measured operating point.

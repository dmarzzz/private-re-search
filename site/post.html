One objective, three coupled holes: J'(π,σ) as the contract for private agentic search

Search is being re-platformed off humans and onto autonomous agents, and the privacy stack we built for the first regime is being silently inherited by the second. That inheritance is a mistake, but not the obvious one. The mistake is not that the old mechanisms are too weak. It is that we never wrote down the objective they were solving, so we cannot say which of them were general results and which were special-case solutions pinned by one accident of the human cost function.

Here is the claim. Search, human or agentic, is one decision-theoretic optimization. A policy π chooses which query or read to issue from belief state b; a stopping rule σ decides when to halt at time τ; U scores the terminal belief against the information need T; c charges for each action. The object solved is

(π*, σ*) = argmax_{π ∈ Π, σ ∈ Σ}  E[ U(b_τ, T) − Σ_t c(b_{t−1}, a_t) ]

where b_t is a posterior over the latent need T updated by the RESULT transition, and Π, Σ are the admissible policy and stopping classes. Call this objective J.

Before privacy enters, fix what the secret is, because two different secrets get conflated and they do not compose the same way. Let S = (ID, T): an identity ID and a latent intent T. The trace O(τ) an adversary observes can leak about either. Identity leakage given known content is an unlinkability problem; intent leakage given a known identity is a confidentiality problem. Transport mixing, PIR, and mixnets attack the ID channel; intent inversion attacks the T channel. A mechanism can shut one channel while doing nothing to the other, and that asymmetry is load-bearing below: PIR kills the ID channel and leaves the T channel untouched, which is exactly why the stopping rule, the one control that touches the T channel, matters.

Privacy is not external to J. It enters either as a leakage cost ℓ(a_t, O_<t), the marginal information the next action adds about the chosen secret given the trace so far, folded into the running cost,

J'(π, σ) = E[ U(b_τ, T) − Σ_t ( c(b_{t−1}, a_t) + λ · ℓ(a_t, O_<t) ) ]

or as a hard constraint on the live trace, I(O(τ); ID) ≤ ε for the unlinkability secret. Throughout, ℓ is leakage about whichever component of S a given claim names; we say which.

The privacy and stopping guarantees engineered for human search were the argmax of J at the human cost vector: attention, impatience, self-censoring sparsity. Substitute the agent cost vector (tokens, compute, money, near-zero latency-intolerance, machine-regular query density) and the objective is unchanged while its argmax is recomputed wholesale. Some guarantees transfer. Some break. Some become newly feasible. And the two terms that decide privacy (ℓ) and termination (σ) turn out to be coupled, not separate stacks.

Why now. Three terms of J' moved at once in the last eighteen months. The cost vector moved empirically: agentic search is now measured at scale (Ning et al. 2026 report 14.44M real agentic search requests across 3.97M sessions). The attack term caught up to the agent specifically: intent-inversion attacks (IntentMiner, 2025) recover roughly 85% semantic alignment with a user's sensitive intent from semi-honest tool-call logs without the prompt. That is reconstruction of T up to paraphrase, not exact recovery and not identity linkage, and it is enough to make the leakage term a well-posed target: if an attacker can recover ℓ on the T channel, a defender estimator is a problem worth posing. It does not yet make the defender's online bound solved (Q2). The defense term crossed buildability: Tiptoe does cryptography-only private web search over 360M pages at single-digit-second online latency; Oblivious HTTP is RFC 9458 and in production for Safe Browsing; Nym-style mixnets ship. The human-search privacy equilibrium was pinned by human impatience, and that constraint is being removed now, before agent stacks settle their transport, retrieval, and credential defaults.

Goals

Every goal is a checkable deliverable against the unification claim.

  1. Pin the frame and exhibit the projections. Write the tuple P = ⟨B, b0, T, A, RESULT, U, c, π, σ⟩ and recover AI state-space search (f = g + h), decision-theoretic optimal stopping (Weitzman's reservation value, Wald's SPRT, the secretary/Bruss odds rule), and IR ranking (Robertson's Probability Ranking Principle, BM25) each as a parameter setting of argmax J under its own stated assumptions (Robertson's PRP, for instance, is a theorem under term independence and linear utility; it is a projection only where those hold). Test: the human-to-agent shift preserves the search-theoretic structure of the tuple and changes only c. If moving from the human to the agent regime requires altering a term other than c to keep the structure, the unification is false and we say so. (Note that the PRP assumptions are not preserved verbatim across the regime shift; the invariant we test is the structure, not any one closed-form solution.)

  2. Build the transfer/break/feasible ledger as comparative statics on J'. For every mechanism (Tor, Loopix/Nym mixnets, OHTTP/ODoH, the PIR family, query obfuscation, anonymous credentials) and attack (website fingerprinting, flow correlation, query-log deanonymization, intent inversion), record the human-regime verdict and re-derive it under the agent cost vector. Test: each row resolves to transfers / breaks / newly-feasible with the deciding term named. A verdict that cannot be traced to a specific term of J' is a failed prediction. The latency flip must be a sign change in ∂(viability)/∂c, not an analogy.

  3. Make ℓ measurable online. Instantiate ℓ(a_t, O_<t) = I(O_t ; T | O_<t) and produce a defender-side estimator by running the adversary's inference forward at each step. Account cumulatively under differential privacy for continual observation (Dwork et al. 2010), with memoized-noisy answers to repeats (RAPPOR) to block averaging-out. Test: on held-out trajectories, ℓ̂ ranks high-leakage steps above low-leakage ones (measured AUC against a held-out attacker's realized reconstruction), and Σℓ̂ tracks end-of-episode intent-reconstruction risk on the T channel better than any per-query bound.

  4. Ship a benchmark that scores U, c, and ℓ jointly on the same trajectories. The flip is currently an argument from mechanism costs, not a measured operating point. Test: one (U, c, ℓ) triple per episode; a third party can reproduce the latency flip as a measured shift, not infer it.

  5. Characterize the σ–ℓ Pareto frontier and decide whether private stopping stays an index policy. Solve for (π*, σ*) under J' with a fixed cumulative-leakage budget ε, exploiting that σ is itself a leakage control. Test: exhibit either a proof that O_<t conditioning preserves decomposability, or a counterexample showing it destroys it the way correlated valuations make Pandora's Box NP-hard (Chawla et al. 2020). Either outcome is publishable; ambiguity is not.

  6. (Wrapper) Make a fleet-shared U verifiable without a linkage surface. Because reading rewrites U mid-episode, outcome-optimality is unprovable, so the object is process-faithfulness: a proof the agent ran its declared π honestly over a committed corpus snapshot C, verifiable by a principal who never sees the query or trajectory and never re-runs the search. Test: a ZK statement over C that is sound for faithful retrieval (including non-omission) and query-private at once, under blind-issued, rate-limited, one-per-principal credentials, with verification strictly cheaper than re-running, and a designated-verifier variant whose transcript convinces no third party of compliance-or-deviation. If soundness and query-privacy cannot coexist, that impossibility is the deliverable.

Goals 1 and 2 are foundation, achievable now. Goal 3 is the empirical hinge. Goal 4 is the missing instrument. Goals 5 and 6 are the open core.

Already covered ground

This is the settled core, stated plainly so the open problems read as frontier rather than as gaps a literature search would close.

One objective, one tuple. Search is the tuple P = ⟨B, b0, T, A, RESULT, U, c, π, σ⟩ with objective J(π,σ) = E[U(b_τ,T) − Σc]. Three literatures usually taught as separate fields are projections of it. AI state-space best-first search is J with c = path cost, U = goal attainment, f = g + h. Decision-theoretic search with optimal stopping (Stigler's economics of search; Weitzman's Pandora's Box reservation-value index; Wald's SPRT; secretary/Bruss) supplies closed-form σ for special-case J. IR relevance (Robertson's PRP, BM25) is U under a one-shot, zero-cost-of-continuation degenerate σ. The payoff is leverage: who is searching becomes the cost parameter c, not a different problem. This is the load-bearing move and it is settled.

Human vs agent as a cost-vector substitution. Same tuple, different c. Human: attention, time, impatience; latency matters in seconds; reads snippets on information scent (Pirolli & Card's information foraging); sparse, self-censored queries; stops on satisficing. Agent: tokens, compute, money; near-indifferent to latency; reads full text; dense, parallel, machine-regular, functionally-decomposed queries; stops on a budget or confidence gate. This is the claim that the human privacy and stopping stacks were never general; they were J pinned by one cost vector.

The stopping frame. σ is a value-of-computation threshold in the Russell & Wefald / Horvitz bounded-optimality sense: issue the next query iff its expected marginal value of computation exceeds its cost. Closed-form σ exists (Weitzman, secretary/Bruss, Wald, best-arm Track-and-Stop) and we have catalogued why each breaks for a retrieval agent (below). The calibration question is located: whether an LLM's self-estimated confidence (Kadavath et al.'s P(IK)) is calibrated enough to ground a bounded-optimal σ. Settled as a diagnosis; the constructive σ is open.

Privacy is a term in J. The form of this is standard: the privacy-utility tradeoff is routinely written as a Lagrangian with a λ·privacy penalty, and privacy-as-objective-term is the default formulation in the privacy-ML literature. The content is what is ours: which term, that the unlinkability secret is a hard constraint while the intent secret is a soft cost, and that σ is itself a privacy control on the T channel. Privacy enters either as the leakage cost ℓ(a_t, O_<t) (marginal mutual information the next action adds about the named secret given the trace) folded into J', or as the hard constraint I(O(τ);ID) ≤ ε with k-anonymity on the live stream. Accounting must be cumulative under differential privacy for continual observation (Dwork et al. 2010), because a correlated query chain joins many low-leakage sub-queries into a high-leakage whole; repeats get memoized noise (RAPPOR) to block averaging-out. Privacy is extra terms in the objective already governing search, not a layer bolted on.

The latency-tolerance flip (the centerpiece). Re-pricing the same corpus-side mechanisms against the agent cost vector inverts their viability verdict, and not because the mechanisms got cheaper. They invert because the agent's cost function stopped charging for the exact resource each one consumes. Mixnet delay (Loopix/Nym, with the 1/μ latency knob turned up): unusable for a human, deployable for an agent. Tiptoe-class PIR with a pre-fetchable offline phase: same flip, on server compute and latency. Constant-rate cover traffic: same, on bandwidth. Agent-generated decoys: weak for humans (an ML classifier re-separated TrackMeNot's human decoys), strong for agents (machine decoys are in-distribution because the agent controls the generative process). Each of these touches the ID channel. Run the substitution backwards on the attack term and the ID channel gets strictly worse: the denser, jitter-free, functionally-decomposed trajectory is a cleaner website fingerprint. The T channel gets worse too but by a different mechanism, intent inversion (IntentMiner, ~85% semantic alignment) reconstructing T up to paraphrase from semi-honest tool-call logs without the prompt, the agent-native analogue of AOL-style query-log reidentification operating on the live reformulation chain. The two channels are distinct functionals of the trace, not one quantity with a sign flip: the mixnet that helps ID does nothing for T, and the stopping rule that suppresses a query helps T while a fingerprint on the surviving queries still threatens ID. We concede the flip is so far an argument from mechanism costs, not a measured operating point; that gap is owned in the open problems.

The catalog. Transport/unlinkability: Tor/onion routing, Loopix/Nym mixnets, OHTTP (RFC 9458) + ODoH. Private retrieval: Tiptoe, FrodoPIR, SimplePIR, Spiral, Coeus, Pantheon, keyword/batch PIR; PIR-RAG/PRAG over private corpora. Query obfuscation: TrackMeNot, GooPIR, OB-PWS. Anonymous credentials: Privacy Pass, blind-issued tokens. Attacks: website fingerprinting, flow correlation, query-log deanonymization, intent inversion.

The residential-proxy null result. A concrete negative, because it is the common mistake. Residential/rotating proxies look like privacy but are a trust-shift, not a privacy gain: one billing-linked intermediary observes identity and content, the exact inverse of unlinkability. It moves trust; it does not create the cryptographic property. This is the one mechanism the cost-vector substitution flips nothing about, which is the point.

On method. The corpus behind this was produced by a hybrid loop that is itself an agentic searcher under J: a local research swarm issued queries, fetched and archived raw sources, and multi-agent workflows then fanned out, adversarially verified claims and citations, and synthesized. That the loop is an instance of the object under study is motivation, not validation.

Roadblocks

Each roadblock is a structural breakage of one term of J'.

1. PIR and private RAG do not yet close at web scale (the c term). The flip re-prices latency to near zero but leaves the compute bill on the books. Single-server PIR (SimplePIR/FrodoPIR/Tiptoe-class) buys low online latency with a client hint linear in corpus size that must refresh on corpus change; a live web index churns continuously, so amortization fights the update rate, not the query rate. Server work is still linear per query (touch every record or leak the access pattern), and the agent c explicitly charges for money. Private RAG is strictly harder than private lookup: you owe a private nearest-neighbor search over an embedding index, not a private key fetch, and over a private corpus the "public database" assumption is gone. Multi-server PIR removes the linear-hint problem but imports a non-collusion assumption. And the patient-agent move that makes PIR newly viable, amortizing the offline hint across a fleet, reintroduces a privacy surface on the ID channel: a shared hint is a correlation that can relink fleet members the per-query protocol was built to separate. Sublinear private keyword search at 10^9 is an active systems frontier owned by the PIR community; the term here is the linkage surface the amortization creates, not the scaling result. Retired by a measured operating point reporting online latency, hint-refresh cadence under realistic churn, and per-query dollar cost jointly.

2. Agent stopping has no grounded σ (the σ/U terms). A bounded-optimal σ needs a calibrated estimate of how much the next query moves the answer; LLM self-confidence (P(IK)) degrades exactly where retrieval agents live (long horizons, tool-mediated evidence, OOD needs). Every closed-form rule we would borrow dies on four breakages at once: options correlated with unknown F_i (the condition that makes Pandora's Box NP-hard, Chawla et al. 2020), non-stationary U (reading rewrites belief mid-episode), an unbounded combinatorial action space ("issue one more query"), and an uncertain need T so that value-of-computation is estimated against a moving target and stopping-confidence decouples from answer-correctness. The last is the dangerous one: the agent can be calibrated on the wrong question. And in J', a miscalibrated σ overspends both compute and privacy, because every suppressed query is one that never joins the leakage budget.

3. Budgeting over a correlated stream (composition of ℓ). Agent queries are a reformulation chain, not i.i.d. draws, so per-query bounds do not compose by addition. The correct accounting is DP under continual observation (Dwork et al. 2010); repeats invite an averaging-out attack defeated only by memoized noisy answers (RAPPOR), which couples the privacy layer to agent state and breaks the stateless-transport abstraction. ℓ has no closed online estimator. A fixed budget ε over an unbounded episode forces a spend schedule coupled to σ: spend early and run dry before convergence; spend late and leak before you know it mattered.

4. No benchmark scores J directly. Existing IR benchmarks score U alone; stopping benchmarks score steps; privacy lives on separate testbeds. Nothing scores U, c, ℓ on the same trajectories, so the frontier you would plot has never been instrumented. Scoring ℓ requires an adversary in the loop, so leakage is a number relative to a stated attacker, not a single number. And with non-stationary U and uncertain T, even ground-truth labeling is contested, which is why the wrapper retreats to process-faithfulness.

5. Adoption and the residential-proxy trust trap (the incentive layer). Private search costs strictly more, and the party that pays (the operator) is not the party that benefits (the user). This is public-goods underprovision: privacy is non-excludable across a fleet. The market's current answer is the trust trap: residential proxies look private and quietly fail unlinkability, and they are cheap and already default. Multi-server PIR and mixnets need multiple non-colluding operators plus cover traffic, a coordination good whose strength is the weakest collusion assumption, and the incentive to defect (log and sell) is exactly where the proxy market sits. The window is finite; if the default calcifies to "billing-linked proxy + plaintext index," the public-good version never ships.

6. Adversarial and epistemic integrity (the wrapper). Intent inversion is double-edged: attacker-feasibility on the T channel makes the defender's estimator a well-posed target (Q2), and the same density that enables it makes the agent's trajectory a cleaner fingerprint on the ID channel than any human's. Corpus manipulation is unobservable from inside U: a coalition that poisons the index changes what the agent finds without changing its belief that it searched well, so outcome-optimality is unprovable. The right relaxation is process-faithfulness, with a sharp kernel, proving non-omission (no matching document was withheld) without revealing the selecting predicate, and proving it is open. The credential is the new linkage surface: anti-Sybil issuance is exactly where identity can re-attach, rebuilding the proxy trap one layer up. And collusion-resistance is the strong-form ask: process-faithfulness is only worth it if a content-producer coalition cannot even verify whether the agent complied with a bribe, or the bribe is enforceable and the market collapses to pay-to-rank.

These are not six independent stacks. σ suppresses queries before they reach the leakage budget; the budget cannot be spent without an online ℓ̂; estimating ℓ is running the adversary forward; none of it tunes without a joint U/c/ℓ benchmark, at a compute cost the corpus mechanisms can meet, against an incumbent that fails silently. The hardest roadblocks live where two terms meet: σ × ℓ, and U × verifiability.

The open problems

These are unsolved, coupled terms of one objective. Each isolates one term and states what is not known.

Q1. Is private optimal stopping still an index policy?

Instantiate ℓ(a_t, O_<t) = I(O_t ; T | O_<t), so the budget is a continual-observation DP constraint Σ_t I(O_t;T | O_<t) ≤ ε composing across a correlated stream. Does a Weitzman reservation-value rule survive when the cost of opening box i includes I(O_i ; T | O_<i), a term that depends on every prior open? Decide whether the O_<t conditioning destroys decomposability, so (π*,σ*) is no longer a per-box index but a coupled dynamic program.

Why hard. Weitzman's index is optimal precisely because each box's reservation value is computable in isolation; the policy decomposes because option values are independent. Privacy breaks that independence: marginal leakage is a function of the path taken to reach a query. This is structurally the coupling that turns Pandora's Box NP-hard under correlated values (Chawla et al. 2020), now arising from the observer's posterior rather than the searcher's. The reservation value would have to be indexed by the adversary's belief, which the policy's own past choices update, a fixed point.

The conjecture to disprove. The index survives iff the marginal leakage I(O_i ; T | O_<i) is path-independent given the current belief, i.e., the adversary's posterior is Markov in the belief state so that the reservation value depends on history only through b_t. Where that holds, (π*, σ*) remains a per-box index and private stopping is a deployable threshold. Where the adversary's posterior carries path information the searcher's belief does not summarize, the reservation value is indexed by an object the policy's own past updates, the fixed point is genuine, and the problem is a coupled DP with no index.

Why it matters. If the index survives, σ and ℓ separate and private stopping is a threshold. If not, "add crypto to a stopping rule" is provably the wrong decomposition and the field needs an approximation guarantee for the coupled DP. Either answer settles whether σ and ℓ can be separate stacks; this is the thesis in miniature. Attackable on a two-box linear-Gaussian instance: ℓ under a linear-Gaussian observer, check whether the optimal opening order depends on history beyond the current belief.

Q2. Online estimation of ℓ: run the adversary forward as the defender.

I(O_t ; T | O_<t) has no closed-form online estimator. Construct a defender-side ℓ̂_t that runs the best deanonymizer/intent-inverter forward over the candidate next action and the trace so far, and prove a one-sided conservative bound ℓ̂_t ≥ ℓ_t − δ (what a budget needs) under stated assumptions on the inversion model's calibration.

Why hard. Estimating MI to a latent T from a single realized trajectory, online, with no second sample, is the hard regime for MI estimation. The plug-in is to treat the attacker's posterior Pr[T | O_≤t] as ground truth, but the attacker is a learned, miscalibrated model (IntentMiner at ~85% alignment), so the estimator inherits its calibration error exactly where the budget must be conservative. A defender who optimizes against ℓ̂ creates distribution shift the attacker retrains on, an adversarial fixed point, not a static bound.

Why it matters. This is the keystone. The whole flip rests on "intent inversion being feasible for the attacker makes it feasible for the defender." Nobody has written the estimator and its error bound. With it, λ·ℓ̂ is a real cost the planner can price; without it, J' has an unmeasurable term. First sub-result: conditions under which attacker self-consistency (a P(IK)-style check, Kadavath et al.) yields a conservative MI upper bound.

Q3. A lower bound on free suppression by the optimal stopping rule.

The frontier itself, { (E[stopping time], Σ_t ℓ_t) } achievable at a utility floor U ≥ u0, is the evaluation deliverable (Phase 4), downstream of Q1 and Q2. The problem is the bound that makes the curve a theorem: prove a lower bound on the free-suppression fraction, the cumulative ℓ eliminated by the optimal σ alone before any cryptography is spent, as a function of the need's entropy H(T) and the per-query informativeness. A bound, not a curve.

Why hard. It couples a metareasoning object (σ as a VOC threshold) with a cryptographic one (ℓ as MI to an adversary) never put in the same units, and the bound must hold against the worst feasible adversary, so it is a min-over-policies of a max-over-adversaries quantity. It needs ℓ̂ from Q2 and the policy structure from Q1.

Why it matters. It operationalizes the central thesis: spend the cryptographic privacy budget only where metareasoning cannot pre-empt the leak. A nontrivial lower bound means the cheapest privacy primitive in the stack is a good stopping rule, and the crypto budget shrinks by a provable amount rather than an observed one.

Q4. Composition under secret-dependent adaptivity.

Adaptive composition is settled ground: the DP literature has fifteen years of adaptive composition theorems for queries chosen as a function of past answers, and continual-observation DP (Dwork et al. 2010) already handles cumulative release over a stream. None of that is the open problem. The new object is narrow and specific. An agent's next query is a function of the latent need T it is protecting, via the read, so the mechanism's adaptivity is entangled with the secret itself, not merely with past answers. Define a composition bound for Σ_t ℓ_t under this secret-dependent adaptivity, where the choice of a_{t+1} is measurable with respect to a posterior over the very T that ℓ protects, and (separately) block averaging-out on repeats via memoized noise (RAPPOR). Target: a tight Σ_t ℓ_t ≤ g(n, correlation structure, ε_per) strictly below the naive sum, with the gap attributable to secret-dependence.

Why hard. Existing adaptive-composition machinery conditions on the answer transcript; here the adaptivity channel passes through the secret, so the standard reduction (treat the analyst as adversarial but the secret as fixed) does not apply. Continual-observation DP gives O(log^{1.5})-type bounds for monotone streams, but a reformulation chain driven by T is neither monotone nor answer-adaptive in the classical sense.

Why it matters. Without an accountant for secret-dependent adaptivity, every privacy claim is per-query and therefore false at the session level, which is exactly the level the AOL lesson operates at. This is also a candidate to fold into Q1's budget constraint rather than stand alone; it is listed separately because the secret-dependence is its own technical object.

Q5. Verifiable faithful search: soundness of retrieval and query-privacy on one ZK statement.

Because reading rewrites U, outcome-optimality is unprovable; the relaxation is process-faithfulness: the agent proves it executed declared policy π honestly over a committed corpus snapshot C (Merkle / vector-commitment root), verifiable by a principal who never sees the query, the trajectory, or re-runs the search. The sharp kernel is the negative: prove completeness of retrieval under a private predicate, that no matching document in the committed corpus was omitted, without revealing the selecting predicate. Open: can completeness-under-a-private-predicate and query-privacy coexist on one ZK statement over C, with verifier work o(search cost)?

Why hard. The statement must hide the query and prove a property about documents the agent did not return, a negative over the un-returned set, without revealing the predicate that would have selected them. That is the genuine ZK-over-committed-corpus difficulty; corpus commitments are cheap, but a sound proof of non-omission against a private selector is not an obviously clean arithmetic circuit. (Verifier work o(search cost) is a succinctness requirement, standard for any practical ZK statement, not part of the novelty.)

Why it matters. This makes a delegated search market trustworthy. If U is becoming a fleet-shared public good, an unverifiable agent installs a society-scale prior with no one able to audit it; completeness-under-a-private-predicate with private verification is the mechanism that keeps a delegated searcher accountable. It is the precondition for Q6.

Q6. Pay-without-identifying, rate-limit-without-linking, and a non-transferable faithfulness proof.

Design issuance and payment using blind-issued, rate-limited, one-per-principal anonymous credentials (Privacy Pass line), so an agent can pay for PIR/mixnet/retrieval and be rate-limited without the issuer or service linking queries to identity. Two coupled problems: (a) prove credential issuance does not become the linkage surface the rest of the stack eliminated (the inverse failure of residential proxies); (b) construct a designated-verifier proof of process-faithfulness such that no transcript convinces a third party of compliance-or-deviation, so that a content-producer coalition cannot verify whether a bribed agent complied and the bribe is therefore unenforceable.

Why hard. Anonymous payment, rate-limiting, and per-principal uniqueness pull against each other: Sybil-resistance wants strong identity at issuance, unlinkability wants none at spend, rate-limiting wants to count across spends without joining them. Closing (a) requires that issuance epoch, amount, and timing carry no re-linking fingerprint. Closing (b) is a mechanism-design result resting on a crypto primitive in direct tension with Q5's soundness: the proof must be sound and convincing to the principal yet non-transferable, deniable to everyone else, so that the same transcript that proves compliance to the rightful verifier proves nothing to a briber. Designated-verifier and deniability machinery exist; the open question is whether they compose with a completeness-under-a-private-predicate statement without sacrificing the soundness Q5 needs.

Why it matters. This is where private search becomes market infrastructure. If credentials leak linkage, the entire upstream stack is wasted at the last mile. And if process-faithfulness is made non-transferable, the delegated-search market is collusion-resistant by construction: a content-producer cannot pay an agent to bias retrieval because it can never obtain a proof it got what it paid for. That is the difference between a privacy tool and a credibly neutral epistemic substrate, the one place that phrase is earned, because here the technical content (a non-transferable proof) backs it.

The shape of the frontier: Q1 asks whether private stopping indexes and is the climax; Q2 is the keystone estimator the whole flip rests on; Q3 is the bound that turns the σ–ℓ frontier into a theorem; Q4 is the accounting layer for secret-dependent adaptivity (and may fold into Q1's budget); Q5 and Q6 are the wrapper that turns a single private searcher into a verifiable, collusion-resistant, fleet-shared public good, climaxing on Q6(b)'s non-transferable proof.

A plan in phases

Each phase produces an artifact the next consumes; every phase is a statement about one term of J'.

Phase 0 (done): formalize the contract. Pin the tuple and prove the three projections (f = g + h; Weitzman/Wald/Bruss; Robertson's PRP/BM25). Exit artifact: the tuple, the three reductions, the human-vs-agent cost-vector contrast. This makes "who is searching" a value of c, so every later claim is stated once.

Phase 1: map transfer/break/newly-feasible. Hold J' fixed, substitute the agent cost vector, classify each mechanism. Close, not gesture at: the latency flip as the sign of ∂(viability)/∂c for mixing delay, Tiptoe-class PIR, and cover traffic; the attack term run backwards (cleaner fingerprint, intent inversion on the live reformulation chain); decoy re-pricing (machine decoys in-distribution against the classifier that beat TrackMeNot); and the null result (residential proxies as trust-shift). Exit artifact: a verdict table keyed to terms of J'.

Phase 2: locate the structural breakages of σ and ℓ. State which assumption each closed-form solver needs and which term removes it: the four simultaneous breakages of stopping; ℓ's missing online estimator and continual-observation accounting; and the named gap that no benchmark scores U/c/ℓ jointly. Exit artifact: the breakage map.

Phase 3: build the missing primitive (easy half first). Ship the online ℓ estimator as a standalone measurement tool (run the adversary forward as the defender; the patient agent can afford to evaluate the best deanonymizer before issuing). Then stack leakage-bounded planning behind it: plan π under a cumulative budget, treating σ as the first, cheapest leakage control. This retires "private search = add more crypto": spend cryptographic budget only on the residual σ cannot pre-empt. Exit artifact: the estimator plus a planner minimizing Σ_t I(O_t;T|O_<t) under ε while preserving U.

Phase 4: evaluate the flip and the frontier on shared trajectories. Build the joint U/c/ℓ benchmark on real workloads (the 14.44M-request, 3.97M-session distribution) rather than synthetic queries. Confirm or refute each Phase 1 verdict at a real operating point, trace the σ–ℓ frontier under cumulative accounting, and decide the index-survival sub-question of Q1. Exit artifact: measured curves and an empirical frontier with a verdict.

Phase 5: the wrapper. Open (not close) the verifiable-U question: can completeness-under-a-private-predicate and query-privacy coexist on a ZK statement over a committed corpus without credential issuance becoming a new linkage surface, and can the faithfulness proof be made non-transferable so a briber cannot verify compliance?

The methodology is an instance of the theory. The corpus was produced by exactly the loop the theory describes: a swarm that gathers and archives raw sources (the RESULT transition) and multi-agent workflows that fan out, adversarially verify, and synthesize (the π/σ policy with verification as the stopping gate). The swarm is a patient agent searcher with the agent cost vector, and where its σ stopped too early is a reading of the same coverage failure the theory predicts. We run the loop on its own object.

Closing

The argument is narrow on purpose. There is one objective. Human privacy guarantees were its argmax at one cost vector, and the cost vector just changed under us. The right question is not "what new crypto do agents need" but "which terms of J' move which way, and where do two of them couple." Two terms couple hardest: the stopping rule σ and the leakage cost ℓ both act on the same channel, the intent channel T, so a query never issued is a leak never paid, and the residual on the identity channel ID is exactly what PIR, mixnets, and anonymous credentials must cover. Whether that coupling still admits an index policy is Q1, and it is the question to attack first: a two-box linear-Gaussian instance is enough to start. Write the objective down and the holes stop being a vibe about agent privacy and become locatable, coupled, and well-posed. The argmax is being recomputed right now whether or not anyone names the objective. Naming it is the work.

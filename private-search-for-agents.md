# Private search for your agents: what to wire up now, and what is still missing

You run autonomous research agents. They decompose a task into many sub-queries and fire them at search engines, the open web, and model and search APIs. That query stream is not a few self-censored searches a day. It is a dense, machine-regular, intent-revealing trace, and it leaks two separate secrets: who is asking, and what they want. The standard operational fix, routing through a residential proxy, makes the second problem worse, not better.

This post is the concrete version. Here is the stack that actually hides both secrets, the real systems that implement each layer and their shipping status, an honest table of what you can deploy today versus what is still missing, and the few sharp problems a team could start on this quarter. The one structural insight that makes all of it deployable is that your agents are patient where a human is not. That single fact moves the strong privacy tech out of the "too slow to use" column.

## What leaks

Two secrets, two different defenses.

**Who is asking.** Every request carries your egress IP, linkable to your billing identity at the API or search provider. Routing through your own infrastructure does not hide this; the provider sees the source of every call and correlates the whole stream to one account.

**What you want.** The content of the queries, and more importantly their order and decomposition, reconstructs your intent. This is the AOL lesson, sharpened. [Barbaro and Zeller (2006)](https://www.nytimes.com/2006/08/09/technology/09aol.html) showed searcher 4417749 was re-identified as a specific person from query content alone, with IP and username already stripped: a bag of query terms is a quasi-identifier. An agent's trace is a far larger quasi-identifier than a human's, because it emits verbatim slices of its prompt and chains self-referential sub-queries no self-conscious human would ever type in sequence.

The agent-specific threats sharpen both.

**Intent inversion.** You do not have to leak a single sensitive query for the intent to leak. Functional decomposition forces semantic transparency: a sub-query for blood-pressure norms plus a sub-query for low-sodium recipes jointly triangulate a medical condition that no single query stated. [IntentMiner (Yao et al., 2025)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.14166) reconstructs a user's sensitive intent from a semi-honest server's view of step-level tool-call logs, without ever seeing the original prompt, at roughly 85% semantic alignment with the true intent on the ToolACE benchmark. Treat that 85% as suggestive, not settled: it is a single unrefereed preprint reporting one number on one benchmark, and the qualitative claim (the ordered sub-query chain leaks intent the individual queries do not) does not depend on the exact figure. The attack runs on the live reformulation chain, so scrubbing any one query does nothing when the sequence still leaks. There is no human analogue at this fidelity, because humans do not externalize their reasoning as a clean, ordered query plan.

**Query-stream fingerprinting.** Agent traffic is concurrent, low-jitter, and bursty, with none of the reading pauses, mouse telemetry, or hesitation that pad a human session. That regularity is exactly the signal anti-bot systems key on, and [Cloudflare (2025)](https://blog.cloudflare.com/past-bots-and-humans/) reports AI-crawler traffic is now a measurable and growing share of requests and that AI bots are fingerprinted by signature and blocked. The same regularity hands the metadata adversary a cleaner trace: website-fingerprinting classifiers that recover the visited site from encrypted traffic shape ([Panchenko et al., 2011](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/2046556.2046570); [Sirinam et al., 2018](https://arxiv.org/abs/1801.02265), over 90% accuracy against defended Tor) were built against human dwell-time jitter as incidental cover. Remove the jitter and the classifier's job gets easier.

### Why residential proxies are a trap

Because the traffic is trivially bot-classified, the operational pressure is to route through residential proxy pools to borrow the IP reputation of real consumer connections. That is the trap.

A residential proxy solves a delivery problem (does my request get served?) and does nothing for the privacy problem (who learns what I am doing?). The provider's gateway is in-path for every request: your client authenticates to the gateway with an account or API key, the gateway picks an exit and forwards, the response returns through the gateway. So the provider sees your real source identity, your destinations, and your timing and volume for all of your traffic, and can trivially correlate every session to your billing identity. With a standard CONNECT tunnel it reads the destination and the cleartext TLS SNI; with a man-in-the-middle CA it reads content too. You do not remove an observer. You add one, and you pick the worst possible one: a single party that holds both identity and content, with no cryptographic unlinkability. It is the inverse of the unlinkability mechanism that [RFC 9458](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc9458.html) and the [Cloudflare OHTTP proof](https://blog.cloudflare.com/stronger-than-a-promise-proving-oblivious-http-privacy-properties/) call out as the weakness of VPNs and plain proxies.

It is worse for an agent than for a human, on two counts. First, the stream you hand the operator is the dense, intent-revealing trajectory above, the one that is more deanonymizing than a human's sparse stream, delivered in full. Second, the supply side is often a coercive or criminal pipeline. [Resident Evil (IEEE S&P 2019)](https://www-users.cse.umn.edu/~fengqian/paper/rpaas_sp19.pdf) detected roughly 6M residential proxy IPs and found many run on likely-compromised hosts; [911 S5 (DOJ, 2024)](https://www.justice.gov/archives/opa/pr/911-s5-botnet-dismantled-and-its-administrator-arrested-coordinated-international-operation) was a botnet of millions of malware-infected machines sold as residential proxies; Google's January 2026 [IPIDEA disruption](https://cloud.google.com/blog/topics/threat-intelligence/disrupting-largest-residential-proxy-network) sourced IPs through monetization SDKs and trojanized devices, with 550+ distinct threat groups using it in a single week; and the FBI's March 2026 [PSA260312](https://www.ic3.gov/PSA/2026/PSA260312) warns these networks route through compromised home and IoT devices whose owners never knowingly consented. A China-located node is strictly the worst version: it inherits Great Firewall DPI and [TLS-handshake degradation](https://gfw.report/blog/gfw_esni_blocking/en/) on top of the single-trusted-party exposure.

Carry this forward: residential proxies optimize for evading server-side blocking. As anonymity they are an anti-pattern. They relocate the party who can see everything from your ISP and the destination site to a commercial intermediary linked to your billing identity, and provide none of the cryptographic unlinkability the rest of the stack is built to give you.

## Why agents change the calculus

The privacy tech that hides what residential proxies cannot was repeatedly judged too slow for interactive search. That judgment was made against a human's cost function, where latency is paid in attention and decoy queries are paid in irritation. Re-price the same mechanisms against a patient background agent and the verdict inverts.

A person abandons a search that takes seconds. A background research agent does not. It pays in tokens and wall-clock it already spends, not in human attention, and it generates queries for the marginal cost of a few tokens. Three things follow directly.

**Mixnet delay becomes a tunable knob, not a tax.** [Loopix (Piotrowska et al., 2017)](https://www.usenix.org/conference/usenixsecurity17/technical-sessions/presentation/piotrowska) and its production descendant Nym add per-hop Poisson mixing delay by design: each node holds each packet for an independently drawn exponential time, so input and output cannot be linked even by a global passive adversary. The processing overhead is tiny (under 1.5 ms per hop), but the security delay is deliberate and lands end-to-end in the range of hundreds of milliseconds to several seconds. For human chat that crosses the usability cliff. For a background agent there is no cliff: the mean-delay knob that buys stronger anonymity can simply be turned up, because waiting costs the agent nothing.

**PIR compute becomes amortizable, not prohibitive.** Private Information Retrieval returns a result without the server learning which query you asked. Its multi-second online latency, tens of MiB per query, and heavy server bill are a non-starter for an interactive human. For an agent, the offline phase is pre-fetchable during idle time, the online latency is off the interactive critical path, and the bandwidth is a line item you can price.

**Cover traffic becomes free instead of annoying.** Human-realistic decoy generation is hard, and [classifiers re-separate decoys from real queries given short history](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-642-14527-8_2), which is why [TrackMeNot/GooPIR](https://nissenbaum.tech.cornell.edu/papers/HoweNissenbaum.pdf) failed for people. An agent generates contextually plausible, semantically diverse decoys at scale and can sustain constant-rate cover traffic indefinitely, which is exactly the steady dummy traffic that defeats intersection attacks.

The mechanisms did not get cheaper. Your agent's cost function stopped charging for the resource they consume.

## The stack

Three layers, each hiding a different secret, each composing with the others.

### Transport: hide WHO

Route agent egress so the search provider never sees your client IP, and so no single party sees both your identity and your content.

**Oblivious HTTP** ([RFC 9458](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc9458.html)) is the cheapest win and should be the default transport for agent-to-API and agent-to-tool calls. A relay sees your IP but only ciphertext; a gateway sees content but only the relay's IP; de-anonymizing you requires two independently-operated parties to collude. One relay hop, near-zero added latency, repeated requests mutually uncorrelatable. **Status: standardized IETF standards-track RFC.** The same two-hop oblivious split is the model behind production deployments like Apple Private Relay; OHTTP itself is reported to front consumer lookups such as Safe Browsing, though I am citing the RFC, not a deployment announcement, so verify the specific deployment before you lean on it. Wire this up today. No latency flip required, it was already cheap.

**Mixnet** (Loopix / Nym) is where the patience flip pays off directly. It gives unlinkability of input to output even against a global passive adversary watching every link, which OHTTP's single relay hop does not. The deliberate per-hop delay that made it unusable for human chat is a free anonymity dial for a background agent. **Status: shipping** (Nym is the live network; [Loopix](https://www.usenix.org/conference/usenixsecurity17/technical-sessions/presentation/piotrowska) is the design). This is the strongest available metadata defense, and the layer where the latency-tolerance flip is load-bearing.

### Content: hide WHAT

Private Information Retrieval drives query-content exposure toward zero rather than merely obscuring it. This is the strongest answer to intent leakage.

| Target | Mechanism | Shipped example | Cost reality |
|---|---|---|---|
| Public web search | PIR over a precomputed semantic index | [Tiptoe (Henzinger et al., 2023)](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3600006.3613134): cryptography-only private nearest-neighbor search over ~360M pages | 2.7 s online latency, ~145 core-seconds server compute, 56.9 MiB per query, ~74% of which is a query-independent offline phase |
| Your own corpus (RAG) | PIR-RAG / private vector search | [PIR-RAG (Wang et al., 2025)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.21325), unrefereed preprint, author-reported | ~16.8 s end-to-end on MS MARCO including document fetch |
| Private query *and* private corpus | MPC similarity search | [PRAG (Zyskind et al., 2023/2024)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.12955), PrivateNLP @ ACL 2024: hides both query vector and database, sublinear communication | Workshop-scale, not yet web-scale |

The underlying single-server PIR primitives are shipped and documented: [SimplePIR/DoublePIR](https://www.usenix.org/conference/usenixsecurity23/presentation/henzinger), [FrodoPIR](https://petsymposium.org/popets/2023/popets-2023-0022.pdf), [Spiral](https://eprint.iacr.org/2022/368). Related private-retrieval building blocks include [Coeus](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3477132.3483586) (oblivious ranking) and [Pantheon](https://www.vldb.org/pvldb/vol16/p643-ahmad.pdf) (private keyword retrieval).

Be honest about the blocker: PIR's cost is real. At ~360M pages, single-digit-second online latency and tens of MiB per query is a non-starter for an interactive human. For your agent it is not. Tens of seconds for a private retrieval is a deal-breaker for a person and an ordinary step latency for a deep-research agent already spending minutes on a task.

The honest concession is at the fleet level, and it is worth stating precisely because it is the one genuinely hard crypto problem here. SimplePIR-class schemes split work into a query-independent offline phase and a cheap online phase. The offline phase produces a hint: a large client-side digest of the database that makes the online query small. The single-agent economics work because that hint amortizes over many of one agent's own queries. The temptation at fleet scale is to compute the hint once and share it across agents, since it is query-independent. The problem is that the hint is keyed to a specific database snapshot, so every agent answering against the same shared hint is provably querying the same index version at the same time, which re-links them into one anonymity set defined by the hint epoch; and any scheme that batches multiple agents' online queries against one server pass to share compute makes those queries jointly observable and correlatable by arrival in the batch. You can have a per-agent hint (no cross-agent linkage, no amortization) or a shared hint and batched online work (amortization, but the sharing boundary is exactly a linkage boundary). Squaring that, an offline phase whose cost is shared across a fleet without the sharing structure itself becoming the correlation handle, is the open problem. It is not a reason to skip the layer; it is the thing to fund.

### Access: pay and rate-limit without linking

You still need to authenticate, pay, and clear rate limits. Done naively, that re-introduces the stable identity the first two layers just removed. The fix is anonymous credentials: prove entitlement without revealing which account.

**Privacy Pass** ([Davidson et al., 2018](https://petsymposium.org/popets/2018/popets-2018-0026.php)) lets a client prove it holds a valid token without linking issuance to redemption, so two authorized requests cannot be tied to the same account. **Status: PoPETs 2018 primitive, standardized and deployed.** It has shipped at scale (its original use was issuing unlinkable tokens to clients clearing anti-bot challenges), and at least one search provider is reported to use it for unlinkable search auth, which is the directly relevant precedent; confirm the specific provider before you cite it as a guarantee. Adopt it where your providers support it.

This is the mechanism-design layer, and it is the one that makes the residential-proxy trap avoidable. A proxy clears bot-blocks and rate limits by handing one operator a stable, billing-linked identity. Privacy Pass clears the same gates by proving the agent is authorized without re-acquiring a stable identity at all. It is the honest alternative to the proxy: same gate cleared, opposite trust model.

Compose all three: OHTTP transport, PIR retrieval, Privacy Pass access. Each closes a different leak. Note one cross-cutting trap: even a PIR-protected stream leaks through timing and volume if the agent does not also regularize its emission schedule, which is exactly why the programmatic-timing control an agent has over its own traffic is load-bearing and not a footnote.

## Deployable today vs missing

| Layer | Deployable today | Missing / too expensive |
|---|---|---|
| **Transport (WHO)** | OHTTP as default tool transport (RFC 9458 standards-track, two-hop oblivious model already in production-scale deployments); Nym for global-passive-adversary-grade metadata hiding (latency is free for a patient agent) | A turnkey private-search proxy/SDK an agent points at, bundling OHTTP-or-mixnet transport with the access layer; an independently-operated relay/gateway split (run both yourself and you have rebuilt a VPN); no large public OHTTP relay pool for arbitrary destinations yet |
| **Content, public web (WHAT)** | Private web search (Tiptoe) over 360M pages at single-digit-second online latency; PIR primitives are shipped libraries | A Tiptoe-grade index operated as a service you can point an agent at; web-scale, continuously-refreshed corpus whose offline + bandwidth cost amortizes across a fleet without reintroducing linkage |
| **Content, own corpus (WHAT)** | Private RAG (PIR-RAG) and corpus-private MPC search (PRAG) at double-digit-second latency over a fixed modest corpus | Independent replication of the preprint numbers; usable cost at production corpus size and update frequency; sharing the offline work across agents without re-linkage |
| **Access (pay/rate-limit)** | Privacy Pass for unlinkable auth (a standardized, deployed primitive; reportedly in use for search auth) | Rate-limited anonymous credentials that survive agentic cadence (millions of sub-queries, long runs); an issuance flow that does not itself become the linkage point; provider-side redemption adoption |
| **Not privacy at all** | Residential proxies (delivery/evasion only); Tor for agents (exit-blocking plus fingerprinting make it a wash) | n/a; keep both strictly out of the privacy story |

## What to build

Four things a team could start this quarter. None need new crypto invented. They need known primitives wired into the agent stack, plus honest engineering on the cost numbers. Build item 1 first: it is the cheapest, ships this quarter, and items 2 and 3 bolt onto it. Item 4 is the honest still-a-real-blocker coda.

**1. A turnkey private-search proxy and SDK the agent points at. Start here.** One endpoint an agent's HTTP client and tool layer route through. It terminates OHTTP so the search provider and tool APIs never see the client IP, regularizes the emission schedule (batching, jitter, constant-rate padding) so the machine-cadence burst pattern stops being a fingerprint, and exposes anonymous-credential redemption (item 2) for metered endpoints. The agent author changes a base URL, not their code. The hard part is operational, not cryptographic: OHTTP's unlinkability requires a relay and a gateway run by distinct non-colluding operators, and the proxy has to present a coherent non-bot client stack (TLS and HTTP/2 fingerprints) end to end, not just hide the IP. This is the highest-leverage win and the one to ship first: it closes the WHO leak today with a base-URL change, and it is the direct, honest replacement for the residential proxy this post spends a full section demolishing. Same gate cleared (bot-blocks, IP reputation), opposite trust model (no single party holds identity and content). The next two items are upgrades that bolt onto this proxy.

**2. Unlinkable metered access via anonymous credentials (bolts onto item 1).** A way for an agent to prove it is entitled (paid, rate-limit-cleared, not a bot) without revealing which account is asking, exposed through the proxy's credential-redemption path. Privacy Pass is the primitive and it is already deployed at scale; build the issuance and redemption flow for agent-cadence traffic, where an account buys a budget, redeems unlinkable tokens per query, and the provider meters on tokens it cannot tie back to the buyer. Rate-limiting and anonymity pull against each other, so the scheme has to enforce a real per-account budget while keeping redemptions mutually unlinkable at agentic burst rates, and the issuance itself becomes a linkage point if timing and batching are careless. The metering primitive (prove "I have budget left" without revealing which account, unlinkably across thousands of calls) is the concrete crypto-and-engineering problem to build here. One measurement discipline to fold in while building it: score your own deployment by running the adversary's inference as your metric (intent-reconstruction alignment in the style of IntentMiner, website-fingerprinting accuracy, IP-linkage) on real agent trajectories such as the [Agentic Search in the Wild](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.17617) corpus, so the privacy claim is a measured operating point, not an argument from mechanism costs.

**3. Mixnet egress for the patient path (bolts onto item 1).** Route the agent's non-time-critical egress (background research, scraping, bulk retrieval) over a mixnet (Loopix / Nym), behind the OHTTP path, and expose the per-hop mean-delay knob as a per-task tunable. Seconds of mixing delay is fine for a background agent and buys defense against the global passive metadata adversary that OHTTP's single hop does not. The hard parts: mixnet egress to the open web still hits exit-reputation and bot-blocking, the agent's machine-regular traffic is more fingerprintable than a human's once you strip involuntary human timing (so this must pair with the schedule-regularization of item 1), and choosing the delay knob per task is a real policy problem (too low loses the anonymity, too high blows the latency budget). This is the layer where the patient-agent insight pays off most directly.

**4. Private RAG at usable cost over the agent's own corpus (the honest blocker).** A retrieval layer where the agent fetches the document or vector it needs without the index server learning which one, wrapped behind a normal `retrieve(query) -> docs` interface. The building blocks exist (Tiptoe, PIR-RAG, PRAG). The single-agent numbers are fine; the shared-infrastructure economics are not, for the reason made concrete in the Content section: a shared offline hint is keyed to one database snapshot, so sharing it re-links every agent answering against it into one anonymity set, and batching agents' online queries to share compute makes them jointly correlatable. The PIR-RAG figures are author-reported on an unrefereed preprint and need independent replication before anyone budgets against them. This is the one item that is a real research blocker rather than plumbing; treat "private RAG at production index scale and shared-fleet cost without re-linkage" as the headline crypto-and-systems problem worth funding, not a solved checkbox.

## What we already have

This stack is not speculative. The mechanism analysis behind it, and the residential-proxy finding that motivates it, were themselves produced by running research agents over the primary literature: every numeric and factual claim was adversarially verified against primary sources, with overstated or mis-attributed claims corrected and flagged. Two reports back this post. One works through [private search when the searcher is an agent](./private-search-when-the-searcher-is-an-agent.md): the WHO-versus-WHAT split, the IntentMiner intent-inversion result, the latency-tolerance flip, and a per-mechanism viability table re-scored for a patient agent. The other documents [why residential proxies are a trust-shift, not a privacy gain](./anonymized-scraping-residential-proxies-privacy.md): the bot-blocking pressure that pushes you toward them, the supply chain behind them, and the trust-model comparison that puts Tor, OHTTP, and PIR on one side and proxies and VPNs on the other.

The shipped systems are real and named above: OHTTP is a finished IETF standards-track RFC whose two-hop oblivious model is already in production, Nym is a live mixnet, Tiptoe does cryptography-only private web search over hundreds of millions of pages, and Privacy Pass is a standardized, deployed unlinkable-token primitive. The gaps are concrete engineering, not missing theory, with one exception: fleet-scale PIR without shared-offline-work re-linkage, the one genuine research problem, named in the Content section and in build item 4.

## Close

The leak is two secrets, who and what, and the residential proxy you would reach for handles neither; it just relocates the all-seeing observer to a billing-linked intermediary. The stack that does handle both already exists in shipped form at the transport, content, and access layers. The only thing that changed is the cost function: an agent pays in compute and patience where a human paid in attention, so the leakage term that was always too expensive to drive down is now affordable. Ship the OHTTP private-search proxy this quarter as the honest replacement for the residential proxy, bolt unlinkable credentials and mixnet egress onto it, and treat one thing as the real research problem worth funding: PIR over a shared fleet without the shared offline work becoming the linkage that the rest of the stack exists to remove.
